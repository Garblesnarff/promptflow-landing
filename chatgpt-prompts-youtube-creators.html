25 Free ChatGPT Prompts for YouTube Creators (Scripts, Titles, Growth) 2026

By PromptFlow April 2026 9 min read Free to use
The YouTube creators growing fastest in 2026 are using AI for the parts that drain time without requiring your creative voice: title testing, script structure, thumbnail copy, Shorts repurposing, and content planning. These 25 prompts handle all of that. Replace anything in [BRACKETS] with your channel-specific information. Works with ChatGPT (free or Plus), Claude, and Gemini.

💡Video Ideas & Title Optimization (Prompts 1–6)

Prompt 1
My YouTube channel is about [describe your niche/topic in 1-2 sentences]. My target audience is [describe: age, interests, skill level, why they watch]. My channel size is approximately [X subscribers] and my best-performing videos are about [describe your top 2-3 topics]. Give me 15 video ideas that: (1) target search terms my audience is actively searching, (2) have a clear hook and viewpoint (not just "how to X" — videos with an angle perform better), (3) mix evergreen content (search-driven) with timely content (trending topics in my niche), (4) are achievable with my current resources (no studio required). For each idea, suggest both the topic AND a working title I could test.
Generic "video ideas" prompts produce generic ideas. Giving AI your channel specifics and best performers lets it pattern-match to what actually works for your audience.
Prompt 2
I'm making a video about: [topic]. Here is my current working title: "[current title]". Write 10 alternative titles that each follow a different proven YouTube title formula: 1. Number list ("X Things That...") 2. Curiosity gap ("Why I...") 3. Mistake/warning ("Stop Doing This...") 4. Comparison ("X vs Y...") 5. Time-specific ("How I Did X in Y Days") 6. Contrarian ("The Truth About X") 7. Beginner/advanced split ("X for Beginners" / "X You're Too Advanced to Know") 8. Personal story ("I Tried X for 30 Days...") 9. Direct benefit ("How to X Without Y") 10. Question ("Is X Actually Worth It?") For each title: rate how clickable it is (1-10) and note which audience emotion it targets (curiosity, fear of missing out, pain avoidance, aspiration, etc.).
Titles are the most underoptimized part of most YouTube videos. Testing multiple formulas upfront — before you publish — is free. Changing titles after publishing rarely recovers a video that started slow.
Prompt 3
My video is about [topic] and my target audience is [describe]. The video is [X] minutes long. Write 5 YouTube descriptions for this video. Each description should: - Start with 2-3 sentences that summarize the value of the video (visible before "Show More") - Include a natural mention of 3-5 keywords someone would search to find this video (don't keyword stuff — weave them in) - Include timestamps in the format: 00:00 - [section name] (I'll fill in actual times) - End with 2-3 CTAs: subscribe, related video, lead magnet or product (optional) My keywords to target: [list 3-5 keywords you want to rank for] My channel link: [your link] Related video to link: [title or URL]
Most creators write descriptions in 30 seconds. Descriptions affect both search ranking and suggested video placement — a well-written one is 10 minutes of work that improves performance for the video's entire lifespan.
Prompt 4
I need YouTube tags for a video about [topic]. My channel niche is [describe]. Target audience: [describe]. Give me 20 YouTube tags in these categories: - 5 broad tags (high search volume, more competitive) - 8 medium tags (specific to my topic — what someone who knows what they're looking for would search) - 5 long-tail tags (very specific phrases — lower volume, easier to rank) - 2 channel brand tags (your channel name or recurring series) Format them as a comma-separated list I can paste directly into YouTube Studio. Explain briefly why the 3 most important tags will help this specific video rank.
Tags are less powerful than they were 5 years ago but still contribute to YouTube's understanding of your video's topic — especially in the first 48 hours after publication. Spend 5 minutes on them.
Prompt 5
I want to create a series of videos on [broad topic] for my [niche] channel. My audience is [describe]. Design a 6-part series: (1) Title for the series + a tagline that makes it sound compelling, (2) A logical progression for the 6 episodes (each builds on the last OR each covers a different sub-topic), (3) For each episode: working title, 1-sentence premise, what the viewer will be able to do after watching, (4) Which episode should be published first to hook new viewers, (5) How to connect the episodes together (end cards, playlists, description links). The series should be something a viewer would want to watch all the way through — not just 6 loosely related videos.
Series videos dramatically increase session time (YouTube's most important metric) because viewers naturally continue watching. A well-designed series compounds your SEO — each video ranks for the series topic, not just its own title.
Prompt 6
My video on [topic] is underperforming. Published [X days] ago. Current stats: [views], [CTR if known], [average view duration if known]. Title: "[current title]". Thumbnail text: "[thumbnail text if any]". Diagnose what might be wrong and suggest fixes: (1) Is the title likely the problem? Write 3 alternative titles to test, (2) Is the thumbnail text likely the problem? Write 3 alternative thumbnail text options, (3) Are there first 30 seconds improvements I could make (re-upload)? Give me a stronger hook script for the opening, (4) Should I delete and repost, or keep this version? What's the threshold where reposting makes sense?, (5) What related topic should my NEXT video cover to give this video a boost through the series halo effect?
Most creators abandon underperforming videos. But low CTR and title problems are fixable — especially in the first 2 weeks when YouTube is still testing your video with new audiences.

🎬Scripts, Hooks & Storytelling (Prompts 7–12)

Prompt 7
I'm scripting a YouTube video about [topic]. Video length target: [X] minutes. My channel style is [describe: educational, story-driven, reaction, tutorial, vlog, etc.]. My audience is [describe]. Create a detailed script outline with: (1) Hook (first 30 seconds) — write the actual script, not just "start with a hook", (2) Intro promise — tell the viewer exactly what they'll get by watching, in 2-3 sentences, (3) Main content sections (list them with 1-sentence descriptions), (4) Midroll retention moment — a reason to keep watching placed at the 40-50% mark, (5) Call-to-action section — when and how to ask for subscribe/like, (6) Outro — 30 seconds or less, what to say. My video angle/thesis is: [what unique perspective or argument you're making].
YouTube rewards videos where people watch past the 50% mark. Structure that delivers on its hook — and includes a midroll hook — drives the retention metrics that get your video recommended.
Prompt 8
Write 5 different 30-second opening hooks for a YouTube video about [topic]. My channel is [describe niche]. My audience is [describe]. For each hook, use a different opening technique: 1. Open with a counterintuitive statement 2. Open mid-story ("I had just [situation] when...") 3. Open with a shocking statistic or fact 4. Open with a direct challenge to the viewer ("If you're [doing X], you're making a mistake") 5. Open with a question that makes the viewer feel the gap between where they are and where they want to be After each hook, write the transition sentence that connects it to the video's main topic. Rate each hook's expected audience retention improvement (high/medium/low) and explain why.
The first 30 seconds determine your video's average view duration more than any other single factor. Five options lets you A/B test — or choose the strongest before you even start filming.
Prompt 9
I'm creating a tutorial video on [topic] for [beginner / intermediate / advanced] [audience type]. The tutorial covers: [list the main steps or concepts in order]. Write a full tutorial script that: (1) explains each step clearly without assuming too much knowledge, (2) uses analogies or examples when concepts are abstract, (3) includes natural "here's why this matters" moments after key steps, (4) anticipates the top 2-3 mistakes viewers make at each step and preemptively addresses them, (5) keeps the pacing tight — cuts anything that doesn't add clarity or engagement. Target length: [X] minutes. My voice style is [casual/professional/conversational — describe]. Write the script in that voice.
Tutorial videos succeed or fail on clarity, not production quality. The "here's why it matters" moments are what separates tutorials that get shared from tutorials that get watched once and forgotten.
Prompt 10
I need to create a "story-driven" YouTube video about [topic or personal experience]. Here are the key facts of the story: [describe what happened — the situation, what you tried, what went wrong or right, what you learned]. Help me structure this as a compelling narrative: (1) Where should the story start for maximum hook? (Often not the beginning), (2) Create a 3-act structure: Setup (the situation before), Conflict (what went wrong or the challenge), Resolution (what you figured out), (3) At what point should I introduce the "lesson" or main takeaway? (4) Write the opening 60 seconds of the script using this structure, (5) What visual B-roll moments should I plan to film to support each section?
Story-driven videos outperform pure information videos on average view duration because humans are wired to follow stories, not data. The structure makes the difference between a rambling personal video and one that hooks and holds attention.
Prompt 11
I just filmed a video and I'm now writing the script for editing. I want to trim it down from [current length] to approximately [target length]. Here is a rough transcript of what I said: [paste transcript or summarize sections]. Help me identify: (1) Which sections can be cut entirely without losing value, (2) Which sections should be shortened — and what specifically to cut, (3) Where do I repeat myself, (4) What is the "must-keep" core of the video — the sections that, if cut, would hurt the video's value, (5) Write 2-3 tight transitions I can use between sections I'm cutting together in edit.
Over-long videos are the most common cause of low average view duration. Editing for length is brutal to do alone because you're too attached to your own footage. A cold eye on the transcript makes cuts obvious.
Prompt 12
Write a YouTube channel trailer script for my channel about [describe your niche and what makes your channel unique]. Target length: 60-90 seconds. My audience is [describe]. My channel's unique value proposition is [why they should subscribe to you and not someone else covering the same topic]. Structure: (1) Hook — grab new visitors in the first 5 seconds, (2) Problem/pain — what does your audience struggle with that you solve?, (3) What they'll get — 3-4 specific things subscribers get (be concrete, not vague), (4) Social proof — what you've achieved or what past viewers say (or skip if new channel), (5) CTA — subscribe and why right now. Write this in spoken-word style, as if I'm talking directly to camera. Conversational, not corporate.
Channel trailers are shown to non-subscribers and are the highest-converting piece of content on your channel. Most creators use the same trailer for years — a strong one is worth writing carefully once.

📱YouTube Shorts & Content Repurposing (Prompts 13–18)

Prompt 13
I have a long-form YouTube video about [topic]. Here is a summary of the video's main points: [paste your key points or transcript summary]. Identify 5 Shorts ideas from this video: (1) Which specific 30-60 second moment from the video would make the strongest Short? (identify by topic/moment), (2) For each Short: write the opening line (hooks are more important on Shorts than anywhere else), suggest the on-screen text/caption to overlay, and write the last 5 seconds (Shorts need a hard loop or re-watch hook), (3) Which Short is most likely to attract subscribers who then watch long-form? (4) Should any of these Shorts link to the long-form video? If yes, suggest the CTA.
Shorts and long-form serve different algorithmic purposes — Shorts grow new subscribers while long-form monetizes them. Repurposing long-form into Shorts is 4x more efficient than creating Shorts from scratch.
Prompt 14
Write a 45-second YouTube Short script about [topic]. My niche is [describe]. This Short should: (1) hook in the first 3 seconds — write the exact opening line, (2) deliver one specific, valuable insight or tip (not a list — one thing done well), (3) end with either a loop hook (makes them watch again) or a curiosity hook (makes them want the long-form). Style: [fast-paced and punchy / educational step-by-step / personal story / reaction]. No lengthy intro, no "hey guys, welcome back," no subscribe reminder in the first 30 seconds.
Shorts fail most often because creators apply long-form structure to a 45-second format. The rules are different: value in second 1, loop or forward-hook in second 45.
Prompt 15
I want to repurpose this YouTube video content into other formats. Here is the video topic and main points: [describe video]. Create a repurposing plan for: (1) Twitter/X thread — what are the 5-7 tweet-sized insights I can pull?, (2) LinkedIn post — which single idea from the video would resonate most with a professional audience? Write it., (3) Instagram carousel — what are 5-8 slides I could create?, (4) Email newsletter — write a 150-word email that uses the video as the main value and links out, (5) Pinterest pin — write 3 pin descriptions that link to the video (not the channel — the specific video). For each format, tell me how much to adapt vs. how much to copy directly.
One video = one piece of content is a massive waste. The research, thinking, and production already happened — distribution across 5 channels costs 30 minutes of writing, not 30 more hours of production.
Prompt 16
Write 5 YouTube Community Post ideas for my [niche] channel. My audience is [describe]. My most recent video was about [topic]. Each community post should serve a different purpose: (1) Drive views to recent video (write the post), (2) Gather audience feedback on upcoming content (write a poll or question), (3) Share a quick tip that's too small for a full video but adds value, (4) Behind-the-scenes or personal update that builds connection, (5) Question that generates comments and engagement (make it easy to answer — not a 3-part essay prompt). Write the full text for each post. Under 150 words each. Include any emoji or formatting that fits the post.
Community posts reach subscribers who missed your last video and signal to YouTube that your channel has engaged, active subscribers — which feeds into suggested video placement.
Prompt 17
Help me create a 30-day YouTube content calendar for my [niche] channel. My current upload frequency is [X per week]. My audience is [describe]. For each week of the month, plan: (1) 1-2 long-form video topics with working titles, (2) 2-3 Shorts ideas derived from the long-form topics or standalone, (3) 1 Community Post concept, (4) The content pillar each video falls into (I want to cover: [list your 2-4 content pillars — e.g., tutorials, opinions, personal stories, case studies]). Make sure the calendar builds toward a cohesive month — not just 30 unrelated videos. Include 1 "evergreen anchor" video per week that I'll promote heavily for SEO.
Consistent posting (same day, same frequency) is one of the clearest patterns in channels that grow. A monthly calendar removes the daily decision of "what should I make" — which is where most upload schedules break down.
Prompt 18
I want to create a YouTube Shorts series that drives people to my long-form channel. My channel is about [topic]. My long-form videos are [describe typical length and format]. Design a 10-Short series: (1) What is the through-line concept? (what makes these Shorts feel like a series, not 10 separate videos), (2) Write titles for all 10 Shorts, (3) Write the opening line for each, (4) How does each Short tease the long-form content without giving it all away?, (5) What is the ideal call-to-action for each Short — link to a specific video, subscribe, or comment? (6) What order should I publish them in to build momentum?
Random Shorts that aren't connected to a channel strategy grow your Shorts views but not your long-form subscribers. A series with a clear through-line and consistent CTAs turns Short viewers into channel subscribers.

📈Channel Strategy & Growth (Prompts 19–25)

Prompt 19
I'm [describe your channel stage: new / at X subscribers / stuck at Y subscribers for Z months]. My channel is about [niche]. My average views per video are [X] and my average watch time is [Y minutes/percentage if known]. Diagnose the growth problem and create a 60-day growth plan: (1) Based on my stats, what is the most likely bottleneck — discovery (CTR/SEO), retention (watch time), or consistency (posting)?, (2) What specific changes should I make to my next 5 videos to test the bottleneck hypothesis?, (3) What content should I prioritize for SEO vs. community/subscriber retention?, (4) Give me 3 "breakthrough video" ideas — topics with high search volume that are achievable for my channel size and could attract 10x my normal views.
Most stuck channels are diagnosing the wrong problem. Spending 6 months optimizing thumbnails when the real issue is watch time is 6 months of wasted effort. This prompt forces a proper diagnostic before prescribing fixes.
Prompt 20
I want to collaborate with other YouTube creators in my [niche]. My channel size: [X subscribers]. My niche: [describe]. Help me: (1) Identify what types of creators I should target for collabs (same size? bigger? complementary niches?), (2) Write a collaboration pitch email that's direct, shows I've watched their content, and makes a specific proposal — not "let's collab sometime", (3) Suggest 3 specific collab video formats that would work for both channels (not just an interview — think about what format maximizes value for both audiences), (4) What should I offer them? (What does a creator your size bring to the table?), (5) How many cold pitches should I send per week to get 1 positive response?
Collabs are one of the fastest ways to grow past algorithmic plateaus because you're borrowing established trust. Most creators never do them because writing the pitch feels awkward. This prompt removes that friction.
Prompt 21
I want to start monetizing my YouTube channel beyond AdSense. My niche is [describe]. Audience size: [X subscribers]. Average views per video: [Y]. My audience is mostly [describe demographics/interests]. Evaluate these monetization options for my specific situation and rank them by potential: (1) Digital products (prompt packs, templates, courses), (2) Affiliate marketing, (3) Brand deals/sponsorships, (4) Memberships/Patreon, (5) Services (consulting, coaching, done-for-you). For my top 2 options: what should my first 60 days of execution look like? What would I need to build or create?
Creators who rely only on AdSense are one algorithm change from losing everything. The right secondary monetization depends entirely on your niche and audience — generic advice doesn't help. This prompt forces channel-specific thinking.
Prompt 22
Help me write an "About" page and channel description for my YouTube channel. My channel is about [topic]. My audience is [describe]. My unique angle or perspective is [what makes your take on this topic different from others]. Write: (1) A 150-word "About" section that tells new visitors exactly who the channel is for and what they'll get, (2) A 1-sentence channel tagline I could use across all platforms, (3) Keywords I should include in the channel description for search discoverability, (4) The right tone — professional/authoritative, casual/friendly, or personal/story-driven? Recommend based on my niche and audience, (5) A 50-word intro I can say at the start of new videos for non-subscribers who find my content through search or suggested.
Channel descriptions are indexed by both YouTube search and Google. Most creators write them in 5 minutes and never update them. A well-written description drives meaningful subscriber conversion from visitors who reach your channel page.
Prompt 23
A viewer left this comment on my video: "[paste comment]". I want to respond in a way that: (1) answers their question or acknowledges their point genuinely, (2) encourages them to watch more content (without being spammy), (3) opens a conversation rather than closing it, (4) sounds like me, not a corporate response. Write 2 versions of a reply — one short (under 30 words) and one detailed (50-80 words). Tell me which you'd recommend for this specific comment and why. Also: is there a way to turn this comment into a future video idea?
Comment responses drive community engagement signals that YouTube's algorithm rewards. They also show potential subscribers that you're an active, accessible creator — which increases subscription rate from channel visitors who read comments.
Prompt 24
I'm planning to post a video on a [controversial / sensitive / potentially polarizing] topic in my [niche]. The topic is: [describe]. Help me think through: (1) What are the legitimate different perspectives on this topic I should be aware of?, (2) What are the most common objections to the position I'm planning to take?, (3) How can I present this in a way that's honest and opinionated without being dismissive of people who disagree?, (4) What disclaimers or nuances should I include to avoid being unfair or wrong?, (5) Is there a framing that lets me make my point strongly without unnecessarily antagonizing my audience? Give me an opening line that signals confidence but not arrogance.
Controversial videos drive massive engagement — but poorly executed ones create backlash that hurts your channel. Pre-thinking the counterargument before you film (not after you post) is the difference between "thought-provoking" and "cancelable."
Prompt 25
I want to create a lead magnet or free resource to build an email list from my YouTube audience. My channel is about [niche]. My audience is [describe]. My most popular video topics are [list top 3]. Design a lead magnet: (1) Suggest 3 specific free resource ideas that would be highly valuable to my exact audience (not "a PDF" — name the specific content), (2) For my top suggestion: what would it contain? Create an outline, (3) Where and how should I mention it in my videos to maximize opt-ins without feeling like a constant pitch?, (4) What should the landing page say? Write a headline and 3 bullet points, (5) What email should they receive immediately after subscribing? Write it (under 200 words).
Email subscribers are 10x more valuable than YouTube subscribers because you own that relationship — YouTube can demonetize, ban, or bury your channel, but your email list is yours. A creator with 10,000 email subscribers and 50,000 YouTube subscribers is more resilient than one with 500,000 YouTube subscribers and no email list.
On using AI for video content: These prompts work best when you bring your own perspective, experience, and voice. AI is great for structure, research frameworks, and first drafts — it's terrible at replacing your specific insight, stories, and personality. Use it for the mechanics; bring your own substance. Audiences subscribe to creators they trust, and trust comes from authenticity that no prompt can generate for you.
