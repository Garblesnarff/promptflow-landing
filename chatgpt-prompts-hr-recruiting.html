20 Free ChatGPT Prompts for HR & Recruiting (Hiring, Onboarding & People Ops) 2026

HR managers and recruiters spend 60-70% of their time on written communication — job descriptions, interview guides, offer letters, performance reviews, policy documents, and employee emails. These 20 prompts cut that time in half. They're drawn from our ChatGPT Business Prompts 500 collection and built for real HR work: fast, specific, and ready to customize. 🔍Hiring & Recruitment (Prompts 1–5)

Prompt 1
Write a job description for a [job title] role at a [company type/size, e.g., "Series A B2B SaaS startup" or "mid-sized regional accounting firm"]. The role reports to: [manager title]. Primary responsibilities: [list 3-5 key responsibilities]. Must-have qualifications: [list 2-4 non-negotiable requirements]. Nice-to-have: [list 2-3 preferred skills]. Compensation range: $[X]-$[Y]. Format: use plain language, avoid corporate jargon, write in second person ("You will..."). Lead with what makes this role compelling — not the company boilerplate. End with the application process (1-2 sentences). Under 450 words total.
Job descriptions written in second person and leading with impact attract 30-40% more qualified applicants than third-person, responsibility-heavy formats. Most job posts bury the interesting work under company history — this prompt flips that order.
Prompt 2
Write a LinkedIn sourcing message to send to a passive candidate for a [job title] role. Their profile suggests they have: [skills or experience you noticed]. What makes this opportunity interesting for someone at their level: [specific aspects of the role, comp, team, or growth opportunity — not generic "exciting company" language]. Message requirements: under 75 words, personalized opening that references something specific from their profile, one clear ask (a call, not a job pitch). Do not use the phrase "I came across your profile." Write 2 variations: one more direct, one more curiosity-based.
LinkedIn InMail response rates drop sharply above 75 words and with generic openers. Two variations let you A/B test tone within the same sourcing campaign without rewriting each time.
Prompt 3
Create a structured interview guide for a [job title] position. The three most critical competencies for this role: [list them — e.g., "stakeholder communication, data analysis, project prioritization"]. For each competency, write: (1) one behavioral question (STAR format), (2) one situational question (hypothetical scenario), (3) two follow-up probes that reveal depth of answer. Also include: a 5-minute opening question to put the candidate at ease, and one question that tests self-awareness (how they'd describe their biggest professional failure and what they learned). Format as a clean interview scorecard with space to take notes.
Unstructured interviews have almost no predictive validity. Behavioral + situational questions with pre-written follow-up probes make interviewers more consistent — and legally defensible in case of a disputed hiring decision.
Prompt 4
Write a rejection email for a candidate who made it to the final round but was not selected. Role they applied for: [job title]. Reason for not selecting (internal only, do not include verbatim): [brief honest reason — e.g., "another candidate had deeper domain expertise," "stronger culture fit," "we had to hire for different skills than we originally posted"]. Requirements: genuine and warm (not a form letter), brief (under 150 words), leaves the door open for future roles if they were genuinely strong, does not give specific feedback unless you're willing to follow up with it. Do not use the phrase "We've decided to move forward with another candidate."
Final-round rejections are the most relationship-damaging part of the hiring process. A well-written rejection from the final round regularly generates referrals and goodwill — because it treats the candidate like a person, not an application number.
Prompt 5
Write an offer letter for a [job title] position. Key terms: Start date: [date]. Base salary: $[X] [annually/hourly]. Bonus target (if applicable): [X]% of base, based on [performance/company metrics]. Equity (if applicable): [X] shares/options, [X]-year vest with [X]-year cliff. Benefits: [brief list — health, dental, PTO, etc.]. Reports to: [manager name and title]. Office/remote policy: [describe]. Include: a warm opening paragraph that reinforces why you chose them specifically, clear conditions of employment (background check, right to work), a deadline to accept (5 business days), and a signature line. Professional but human tone. Not legalese.
Offer letters are often the last thing candidates read before deciding whether to accept. A letter that reinforces their decision — not just lists the terms — reduces last-minute counter-offer losses by making the candidate feel chosen, not processed.

📋Onboarding & People Operations (Prompts 6–10)

Prompt 6
Create a 30-60-90 day onboarding plan for a new [job title] joining [type of team, e.g., "a 12-person marketing team at a Series B startup"]. Their role will focus on: [core responsibilities]. For each 30-day period, outline: (1) the primary learning objectives, (2) the key relationships they should build and why, (3) one tangible deliverable that demonstrates value, (4) what success looks like by the end of that phase. Also include: the top 3 mistakes new hires in this type of role typically make in their first 90 days, and how this plan addresses each. Format as a clean document they'd actually want to read.
New hires who have a structured 30-60-90 day plan reach full productivity 40% faster than those left to figure out their role. The "mistakes new hires make" section is the most valuable part — it prevents the same errors without making the new hire feel defensive.
Prompt 7
Write a first-day welcome email to send to a new employee before they start. Their name: [name]. Role: [title]. Start date: [date]. Manager: [name]. What they should do before Day 1 (if anything): [e.g., complete DocuSign forms, set up laptop, read X]. What Day 1 looks like: [schedule summary]. One genuine thing you're excited about having them on the team (specific to their skills or background, not generic). Keep it under 200 words. Warm, not corporate. Should make them feel like they made the right decision to join.
Start-date regret is real — candidates who accept offers still lose confidence during the gap between signing and starting. A specific, warm pre-boarding email reinforces their decision and reduces early attrition before day one.
Prompt 8
Write a performance review template for [job title] roles at a [company type]. The review covers: [time period — e.g., Q1 2026 or annual]. Sections needed: (1) Self-assessment (3 questions — accomplishments, biggest challenge, one thing they'd do differently), (2) Manager assessment (goal attainment, core competency ratings with narrative, areas of strength, development areas), (3) Goals for next period (3-5 specific, measurable goals), (4) Overall rating with definitions for each level (below expectations / meets / exceeds / exceptional). Tone: direct and developmental, not punitive. Include a note at the top reminding managers that ratings should reflect patterns — not recent events (recency bias warning).
Most performance review templates generate useless ratings because they don't define what each rating level means. Including definitions and an explicit recency bias warning improves rating calibration — and makes the process more defensible if challenged.
Prompt 9
Write a Performance Improvement Plan (PIP) document for an employee in a [job title] role. Performance issue: [describe specifically — avoid vague language like "attitude problem," use observable behaviors like "missed 3 of 5 project deadlines in Q1"]. Expected behavior going forward: [specific, measurable outcomes]. Support being provided: [what the company is offering — coaching, clearer expectations, additional training]. Review period: [X weeks]. Check-in cadence: [weekly/biweekly]. Consequences if performance does not improve: [be direct]. Tone: firm but not punitive — this is a genuine attempt to support improvement, not a pre-termination document (unless it is). Include a signature section for employee and manager.
PIPs that fail are almost always vague. Observable behaviors and specific outcome targets give the employee a real path to success — and protect the company legally if the outcome is eventually termination.
Prompt 10
Write a policy document section for our employee handbook on [policy topic — e.g., remote work, PTO, expense reimbursement, parental leave, workplace conduct]. Company context: [size, industry, state/country if relevant]. Key terms of the policy: [describe what you want the policy to say]. Format requirements: plain English (no legalese), under 400 words, structured with a clear statement of the policy, who it applies to, how it works in practice (with a 1-2 sentence example scenario), and how employees submit requests or exceptions if applicable. End with who to contact with questions.
Handbook policies written in plain English generate fewer HR questions because employees actually read them. A short example scenario ("If you work from home three days per week, here's how it works...") eliminates 80% of the edge-case questions before they're asked.

🤝Employee Relations & Difficult Conversations (Prompts 11–15)

Prompt 11
I need to have a difficult conversation with an employee about [describe the performance or behavior issue — be specific]. What I know about the situation: [relevant context — how long this has been happening, whether it's been addressed before, any contributing factors]. Write a script for the conversation in three parts: (1) how to open without making them immediately defensive (first 60 seconds), (2) how to state the issue clearly using observable facts, not judgments, (3) how to close with clear expectations and a specific follow-up date. Also give me 2-3 responses I might hear and how to handle each one without getting derailed.
Most managers avoid difficult conversations too long because they don't know how to open them. A scripted opening that uses facts instead of judgments dramatically reduces defensiveness — and reduces the manager's anxiety enough to actually have the conversation.
Prompt 12
Write a written warning letter for an employee. Employee name: [name, or use "the employee"]. Role: [title]. Issue: [describe the conduct or performance problem with specific dates and instances]. Previous verbal warnings or discussions about this issue: [yes/no — if yes, describe]. Expected change: [specific, observable behavior change required]. Timeframe: [X days/weeks]. Consequences if the issue continues: [be explicit]. The letter must be: factual (no emotional language), firm but professional, free of language that could be construed as discriminatory, and signed by the manager. Include a signature line for the employee to acknowledge receipt (not agreement).
"Acknowledged receipt" vs. "I agree with this" is a critical legal distinction. Written warnings that use emotionally charged language rather than documented facts are the most common source of wrongful termination claims — this prompt forces factual framing throughout.
Prompt 13
Write an all-company email announcing [organizational change — e.g., a leadership change, a department restructure, a layoff, a policy update, a new benefit, an acquisition]. What's changing: [describe]. Effective date: [date]. Why this is happening: [the real reason — be as honest as appropriate]. What it means for employees in plain English: [describe impact — who is affected, what stays the same, what they need to do differently]. Questions to direct to: [name and email]. Tone: direct, not spin. Employees see through corporate messaging that's all positive framing. Acknowledge difficulty if it exists. Under 300 words.
All-company emails that over-spin bad news destroy trust faster than the news itself. Employees share these emails — the goal is a message that doesn't embarrass the company when forwarded. "Acknowledge difficulty if it exists" is the most important instruction in this prompt.
Prompt 14
Two team members are in conflict over [describe the conflict — e.g., "overlapping responsibilities," "different working styles creating friction," "a specific incident that happened"]. I need to facilitate a conversation between them. Write: (1) separate pre-meeting messages to each person explaining what the meeting is for and how to prepare (acknowledge it will be uncomfortable), (2) an opening statement for the facilitated conversation that sets ground rules and frames the goal as resolution, not arbitration, (3) 5 neutral questions I can use to surface each person's perspective without taking sides. Do not assume fault for either party in any of the language.
The opening statement for a conflict resolution meeting is the highest-leverage moment — it determines whether both parties feel safe being honest. Pre-meeting messages that acknowledge discomfort set realistic expectations and reduce no-shows or defensive posturing.
Prompt 15
Create an exit interview question set for a departing employee in a [job title] role who is leaving for [reason if known — e.g., "a higher-paying opportunity," "personal reasons," "career change," "unknown"]. Goals of the exit interview: understand the real reason they're leaving (not just the stated reason), surface any systemic issues, and leave on good terms regardless of circumstances. Include: 8 questions covering their experience, management relationship, growth opportunities, culture, and suggestions. Also include 2-3 follow-up probes for the most common deflections ("everything was fine, just a better opportunity"). End with a closing question that leaves the door open for alumni referrals.
Exit interviews fail because employees give safe answers and HR accepts them. Follow-up probes for the most common deflections ("better opportunity" often masks a manager problem) are what turn exit interviews into actual intelligence.

📊People Strategy & Culture (Prompts 16–20)

Prompt 16
Write an employee engagement survey for a company with [X] employees in [industry]. We want to measure: overall satisfaction, manager effectiveness, career growth opportunities, team collaboration, and company culture. Create 15 questions: mix of 5-point scale (strongly agree to strongly disagree), multiple choice, and 2-3 open-ended. Include an anonymous submission note. Avoid leading questions (e.g., "How much do you love working here?"). Include one question specifically about whether the employee has considered leaving in the past 3 months — this is the single most predictive retention indicator. Format as a ready-to-send survey with an intro paragraph explaining why feedback matters.
"Have you considered leaving in the past 3 months?" is the most predictive single question in employee retention research. Most engagement surveys omit it because it feels uncomfortable — but avoiding the question doesn't prevent the attrition.
Prompt 17
Write a job posting for our company's careers page that doubles as employer brand content. Role: [title]. Beyond the role itself, I want this posting to communicate: (1) what makes our culture genuinely different (specific, not "fast-paced environment"), (2) what the growth trajectory looks like for someone in this role, (3) one honest challenge about working here (companies that acknowledge a real limitation come across as more credible). Format: open with a 2-sentence hook that speaks to someone currently frustrated in a similar role elsewhere, then role details, then the culture section, then qualifications. Under 500 words.
Job postings that acknowledge one genuine limitation consistently attract more qualified applicants — because they read as honest rather than a sales pitch. High performers are especially attuned to over-polished descriptions and self-select in based on transparency.
Prompt 18
Write a manager announcement email to a team whose manager is leaving and being replaced by [new manager name and background]. Outgoing manager: [name, tenure, why leaving — use only what can be shared]. New manager: [name, background, what they bring to the team]. Transition plan: [brief — overlap period, handover timeline]. What stays the same: [anything that's important to name]. Tone: acknowledges the change is real (don't pretend it's not a disruption), makes employees feel informed rather than blindsided, introduces the new manager with specifics (not just their resume). Under 250 words. Sent from: [HR or senior leader name].
Team morale drops sharply during manager transitions — not because the transition is bad, but because of uncertainty. The single most effective thing this email can do is give specific information. Vague reassurances ("you're in great hands") amplify anxiety; specific plans reduce it.
Prompt 19
Write a compensation philosophy document for a [company stage — startup, scaleup, enterprise] in [industry]. We pay [describe your general approach — e.g., "at the 60th percentile of market for our geography," "above market for technical roles, market rate for operations," "equity-heavy, salary at 50th percentile"]. Cover: (1) how we determine salaries (market data, role level, geography), (2) how and when salaries are reviewed, (3) how equity works and who gets it, (4) what we don't do (e.g., "we don't negotiate — everyone at the same level gets the same range"). Under 400 words. Written for employees to read, not the board. Plain language, no HR jargon.
Companies without a published compensation philosophy spend enormous HR time on individual salary negotiations and lose candidates to companies that are transparent. Publishing the philosophy — including what you don't do — reduces negotiation overhead and attracts candidates who value fairness over gaming the system.
Prompt 20
Write a company values document for [company name] in [industry]. Our values should reflect how we actually make decisions — not aspirational poster content. Things that are genuinely true about how we work: [list 3-5 things that are actually true about your culture, even if imperfect — e.g., "we move fast and sometimes redo work," "we give feedback directly even when uncomfortable," "customer results matter more than internal process"]. For each value: write the name (2-4 words), a 1-sentence definition, a concrete example of what it looks like in practice, and one behavior that would violate it. 4-5 values total. No clichés: do not include "integrity," "innovation," or "customer-first" without a genuinely specific definition.
Most company values are indistinguishable from each other because they're aspirational, not descriptive. Values that include a "what violates this" line are the ones employees actually use to make decisions — because they're specific enough to apply.
Time reality check: The average HR manager spends 15-20 hours per week on written communications — job descriptions, offer letters, performance docs, policy writing, employee emails. These 20 prompts can cut that to 5-8 hours. At any reasonable HR salary, that's $15,000-$30,000 per year recovered and redirected to work that actually moves the business forward.
