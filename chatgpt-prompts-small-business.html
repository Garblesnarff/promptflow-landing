20 Free ChatGPT Prompts for Small Business Owners (Save 10+ Hours/Week)

These 20 prompts cover the highest-leverage tasks: emails, marketing, customer service, sales, and operations. No coding required. Works with ChatGPT (free version is fine), Claude, and Gemini. 📧 Email & Communication Automation (Prompts 1–5)

Prompt 1
I need to write a professional follow-up email to a client who hasn't responded to my proposal. Context: [describe the proposal, what you offered, the price, how long ago you sent it]. They haven't responded in [X days]. My relationship with them: [new prospect / existing client / warm referral]. Write a follow-up email that: (1) Doesn't sound needy or pushy, (2) Adds a small piece of value or new information rather than just saying "checking in," (3) Makes it easy to reply yes, no, or "not yet," (4) Is under 100 words — short emails get read, long emails get deferred.
"Just checking in" is the weakest follow-up phrase in business. Adding a new data point, idea, or question gives the recipient a reason to reply that isn't just politeness — and a short email respects their time.
Prompt 2
I get asked the same questions repeatedly by clients or customers. Here are my top 5 FAQ questions: [list them]. My business: [describe what you do, who your clients are, your average deal size]. For each question, write a professional, warm email template I can paste and send in under 30 seconds. Include a placeholder for the client's name. Each response should: (1) Answer the question directly first, (2) Anticipate the follow-up question they usually ask next, (3) End with a clear next step so the conversation moves forward.
Templated responses get a bad reputation because people write bad templates — vague, formal, and obviously copy-pasted. Good templates are specific, warm, and answer the follow-up question before it's asked. That's what ends email threads fast.
Prompt 3
I need a 3-email onboarding sequence for new customers who just purchased [describe your product or service]. My business: [describe]. What new customers typically worry about: [list their fears or questions]. What I want them to do in the first 7 days: [describe]. Email 1 (immediately): Welcome + what happens next + who to contact Email 2 (Day 3): Quick win or tip that helps them get early value Email 3 (Day 7): Check-in + what to do next + invitation to give feedback Write all 3 emails. Keep each under 200 words. Tone: [warm and personal / professional / casual].
The first 7 days after a purchase determine whether someone becomes a repeat buyer or asks for a refund. Most businesses send one welcome email and go quiet — exactly when anxiety is highest.
Prompt 4
A customer is upset about: [describe the complaint — slow delivery, product issue, miscommunication, etc.]. What actually happened: [give me the honest version]. What I can offer them: [refund / replacement / discount / explanation / nothing additional]. Write a response that: (1) Acknowledges their frustration without admitting fault for things outside my control, (2) Explains what happened in one sentence — not an excuse, just context, (3) States clearly what I'm offering to resolve it, (4) Doesn't invite further argument or negotiation, (5) Ends with a door open if they have more questions. Tone: professional but human — not corporate-lawyer language.
Defensive responses escalate complaints. Acknowledgment + brief context + clear resolution — in that order — de-escalates most customer service issues in one email. "Not an excuse, just context" is the key framing.
Prompt 5
I want to reach out to [X] past clients or customers I haven't spoken to in [6-18 months] to re-engage them. My goal: [get a call booked / offer a new service / share an update / ask for a referral]. Their situation when we last worked together: [describe]. Write a re-engagement email that: (1) References something specific from when we worked together (leave [SPECIFIC DETAIL] as a placeholder), (2) Has a genuine reason to reach out — not just "it's been a while," (3) Makes one clear, low-pressure ask, (4) Is under 120 words — short enough that reading it takes less time than deciding whether to read it.
Past clients are 5× more likely to buy again than a cold prospect — but most businesses never re-engage them. A specific, genuine, short email outperforms any "I was just thinking of you" blast by orders of magnitude.

📣 Marketing on Autopilot (Prompts 6–10)

Prompt 6
I need one month of social media content for my business. Business: [describe what you do, who your customers are, what makes you different]. Platforms: [Instagram / Facebook / LinkedIn / X]. Posting frequency: [X times/week]. Create a 4-week content calendar with: (1) Post topic for each slot, (2) Whether each post is educational, promotional, or social proof, (3) One hook line for each post (the first sentence that stops the scroll), (4) A simple 3-2-1 ratio guideline: 3 value posts per 2 community/engagement posts per 1 promotional post. No need to write all captions — just the calendar framework and hooks.
Most business owners get stuck at "what should I post today." A 4-week calendar removes that friction completely — you wake up knowing exactly what goes out and what the hook is. The 3-2-1 ratio prevents over-promotion that kills engagement.
Prompt 7
I need to write an "about us" page for my business website. Business: [describe what you do]. Founded: [when, by whom]. Who we serve: [your ideal customer]. What makes us different: [your actual differentiator — be specific, not "we care more"]. Results or outcomes we've delivered: [any metrics, testimonials, or stories]. Write an About page that: (1) Opens with the customer's problem, not our founding story, (2) Explains what we do in one sentence a 10-year-old could understand, (3) States our real differentiator without clichés like "passionate" or "dedicated," (4) Uses one brief founder story that makes us human, (5) Ends with a clear call to action. Target length: 300-400 words.
Most About pages lead with "We were founded in 2019 with a passion for..." — which is the least interesting thing to a potential customer. Opening with the customer's problem and what you do about it converts visitors into inquiries.
Prompt 8
I want to write a Google Business Profile / Yelp / Trustpilot review request email to send to customers after they purchase. My business: [describe]. Average customer: [describe]. The outcome or result they got: [describe what a typical customer achieves]. Write a short email (under 80 words) that: (1) Thanks them for their business, (2) Asks for a review in a way that feels natural, not transactional, (3) Makes leaving a review as easy as possible — include [REVIEW LINK] placeholder, (4) Doesn't offer an incentive (that violates most platform policies), (5) Has a subject line that gets opened. Also write an SMS version under 160 characters.
Reviews are the highest-ROI marketing activity for local and small businesses — they're permanent, trusted, and cost nothing except asking. The barrier is asking awkwardly. A short, warm, specific request sent at the right moment (right after a positive outcome) converts at 20-40%.
Prompt 9
I want to create a lead magnet — a free resource I give away in exchange for an email address. My ideal customer: [describe]. Their biggest recurring problem: [describe]. Products/services I sell: [list with prices]. What they need to believe before they'll buy from me: [the shift in thinking required]. Suggest 5 lead magnet ideas that: (1) Are directly useful to my target customer, (2) Take me less than 4 hours to create, (3) Naturally lead someone toward wanting my paid offer, (4) Are specific enough that only my ideal customer would want them. For the best idea: write the title, a 3-bullet description, and a landing page headline.
Generic lead magnets ("Sign up for our newsletter!") don't work. The best lead magnets are hyper-specific — solving one narrow problem for one type of person. Specificity is what makes someone think "that's exactly what I need."
Prompt 10
I want to repurpose one piece of long-form content into multiple formats. The content: [paste your blog post, video script, podcast notes, or just a detailed topic you know well]. Turn this into: (1) 3 social media posts — one educational, one controversial/opinion, one question/engagement, (2) 5 short-form hooks for Instagram Reels or TikTok (first sentence only — the scroll-stopper), (3) One email newsletter intro (150 words max, ends with a link to the full piece), (4) 3 Pinterest pin titles with keyword-friendly language, (5) One FAQ-style website section with 4 questions and answers. Do not just summarize — each format should be independently valuable.
Creating original content for 5 channels is a full-time job. Repurposing one strong idea across formats is sustainable. The key rule: each format must stand alone. A social post that just says "check out my blog" isn't repurposing — it's pointing at repurposing.

🤝 Sales & Customer Service (Prompts 11–15)

Prompt 11
I need to write a proposal for a potential client. Service/product: [describe]. Client: [describe their business and situation]. What they're trying to achieve: [their goal]. What I can deliver: [your offer, timeline, price]. Their likely concerns: [cost / timeline / "will this work for us?" / risk]. Write a 1-page proposal that: (1) Opens by reflecting their problem back to them (shows I understood), (2) Describes the solution in outcomes, not just activities, (3) Lists 3 deliverables max — proposals that list 15 things create scope confusion, (4) States investment (price) clearly without hedging language, (5) Includes a simple yes/no next step — not "let me know what you think."
Long proposals lose deals. A 1-page proposal that demonstrates understanding, focuses on outcomes, and makes the ask clearly wins more than a 10-page document. "Let me know what you think" is the weakest possible close — a clear next step doubles reply rates.
Prompt 12
A prospect just said: "[their exact objection]." My product/service: [describe]. Price: [your price]. What the objection usually really means: [too expensive / need to check with someone / not sure it'll work / not urgent enough]. Write 3 different responses to this objection, each using a different approach: (1) Acknowledge + reframe (show what the cost of inaction is), (2) Ask a diagnostic question (find out if it's really price or something else), (3) Reduce perceived risk (offer a guarantee, trial, smaller starting point, or payment plan). For each response, explain in one sentence why a prospect would respond positively to it.
Most sales objections aren't really about the stated reason. "Too expensive" is often "I'm not sure the ROI is there" or "I don't trust this yet." Having 3 different tools — reframe, diagnose, reduce risk — lets you match the response to the real objection.
Prompt 13
I want to create an FAQ page for my website. My business: [describe]. Products or services: [list them with prices]. Questions I get before people buy: [list 6-8 common pre-purchase questions]. Questions I get after people buy: [list 4-5 common post-purchase questions]. Write an FAQ page that: (1) Answers each question directly — first sentence is the answer, not a hedge, (2) Groups questions into 2 sections: Before You Buy and After You Buy, (3) Adds 2-3 questions I didn't list but should be answering (anticipate concerns I'm ignoring), (4) Ends with a clear CTA for people who still have questions.
An FAQ page is one of the highest-converting pages on a small business website — it addresses the objections people have right before they decide. The "first sentence is the answer" rule is critical: people scan FAQs, they don't read them.
Prompt 14
I want to set up a simple referral system for my business. Business: [describe]. Average customer value (lifetime): [amount]. What my best clients tend to have in common: [describe]. What I could offer as a referral incentive: [cash / discount / free session / gift card / public recognition]. Design a referral program that: (1) Can be run with zero software (just email and a spreadsheet), (2) Makes the ask feel natural, not transactional, (3) Specifies exactly what to say when asking for a referral, (4) Has a simple tracking system, (5) Includes a thank-you protocol for referrals received, whether they convert or not.
Referral programs fail when asking feels awkward. The reason it feels awkward is there's no script, no clear incentive, and no follow-up system. A referral system that runs on email and a spreadsheet is better than a fancy software tool you never use.
Prompt 15
I want to create a "cancelation save" script for when a customer tries to cancel or not renew. My business: [describe subscription or recurring service]. Common reasons people cancel: [list them]. What I could offer to keep them: [discount / pause / downgrade / free extra / different plan]. Write a 3-step save sequence: (1) Immediate reply email acknowledging their request and asking one diagnostic question (don't fight it yet), (2) If they respond with a reason: a tailored response for each of your top 3 cancellation reasons, (3) If they still want to cancel: a graceful offboarding that leaves the door open for return.
Fighting cancellations head-on accelerates them. The diagnostic question approach works because people who want to cancel often just want to feel heard — many will stay if you ask what went wrong and offer a real solution.

⚙️ Operations & SOPs (Prompts 16–20)

Prompt 16
I want to create a Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) for a task I do repeatedly. Task: [describe the task in detail — what it involves, how long it takes, what tools are used]. Who else might need to do this task: [employee, contractor, VA, or just future me]. Write an SOP with: (1) Purpose: why this task matters and what "done well" looks like, (2) When this task runs: trigger, frequency, deadline, (3) Step-by-step instructions written as if for someone new — numbered, specific, no assumed knowledge, (4) Quality checklist: 5-7 things to verify before marking complete, (5) Common mistakes and how to avoid them.
An SOP written for "someone new with no assumed knowledge" is the only kind that survives being handed off. If you'd need to explain something verbally, it belongs in writing. SOPs are the difference between a business that scales and one that only works when you're there.
Prompt 17
I want to write a job description for a contractor or part-time hire. Role I'm hiring for: [VA / social media manager / bookkeeper / sales person / customer service rep]. What they'll do day-to-day: [list the tasks]. Skills required: [list must-haves vs. nice-to-haves]. What I'm paying: [hourly rate or project fee]. Hours per week: [estimate]. Write a job post that: (1) Opens with what makes this role interesting or different, (2) Lists responsibilities by frequency (daily / weekly / monthly) rather than a bullet dump, (3) Distinguishes clearly between required skills and nice-to-haves, (4) Describes what success looks like at 30 and 90 days, (5) Ends with a specific application instruction that filters out people who don't read carefully.
The "application instruction" trick (e.g., "start your application with the word PINEAPPLE") filters out mass-apply candidates who don't read. It sounds silly but it cuts your review time by 80% by surfacing only applicants who actually want this specific role.
Prompt 18
I want to do a quick audit of how I'm spending my time to find where I'm doing work AI could do. For one week I tracked: [describe what you did each day, even roughly — meetings, emails, admin, content creation, sales calls, etc.]. Analyze this and: (1) Identify the top 3 tasks that are best suited for AI assistance, (2) For each task, suggest the specific type of AI prompt that would work, (3) Estimate the time savings per week if I used AI for these tasks, (4) Identify the 1-2 tasks I should NOT delegate to AI (because they require my specific judgment or relationships), (5) Suggest one thing I can stop doing entirely.
Most business owners think AI is for writing, not for their specific work. A time audit analyzed through the lens of AI suitability reliably surfaces 3-5 hours of weekly savings in places the owner hadn't considered — often ops, research, or communications.
Prompt 19
I want to set up a simple weekly reporting system for my business using only free tools (Google Sheets + email). Metrics I want to track: [revenue, new customers, refunds, support tickets, social follows — pick your own]. My current process: [describe — probably "I don't have one"]. How much time I want to spend on this weekly: [15-30 minutes max]. Design a reporting system: (1) A Google Sheets template structure (sheet names, columns), (2) Exactly when to update it and how long it takes, (3) A weekly email summary template I send myself (5 sentences: what happened, what changed, what needs attention), (4) Three "warning signals" — specific numbers that, if crossed, trigger immediate action.
What gets measured gets managed — but most small businesses have no measurement system at all. A 15-minute weekly review on one spreadsheet, with pre-defined warning signals, gives you more control than any dashboard you pay for but never configure.
Prompt 20
I want to create a simple "AI starter kit" for my business — a list of 5-7 recurring tasks where I'll use AI every week. My business: [describe]. Main time consumers: [list your top 5 repetitive tasks]. Tools I'm willing to use: [ChatGPT / Claude / Gemini / one I pay for]. Create my personal AI Starter Kit: (1) For each of my top tasks: the exact prompt structure I should use (leave [VARIABLES] for me to fill in), (2) Which AI tool is best for each task and why, (3) How to save these prompts so I can access them in under 10 seconds, (4) A "before AI / after AI" time estimate for each task, (5) One rule for when NOT to use AI for a task.
Most people try AI once, get a mediocre output, and conclude it's not ready. The owners saving 10+ hours/week treat AI like a power tool with a fixed workflow — not a magic button they hit when stuck. A personal starter kit with saved prompts is the difference between those two experiences.
