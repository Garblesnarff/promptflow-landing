20 Free ChatGPT Prompts for Coaches & Consultants (Client Work, Content & Proposals) 2026

By PromptFlow April 2026 7 min read Free to use
Coaches and consultants lose 30-40% of their billable hours to admin: intake forms, session notes, proposals, follow-up emails, social content. These 20 prompts are taken from our AI Client Onboarding Template and ChatGPT Business Prompts 500 — designed specifically for knowledge-based service businesses. Each prompt handles one high-frequency coaching task. Replace anything in [BRACKETS] with your specifics.

🔍Discovery & Client Onboarding (Prompts 1–5)

Prompt 1
Create a discovery call script for a potential coaching client. My coaching focus: [describe your niche — e.g., "executive leadership for first-time managers," "business growth for service-based entrepreneurs"]. Structure the call in 4 parts: (1) open with their situation in their words (2-3 questions), (2) understand what they've already tried, (3) quantify what's at stake if nothing changes, (4) close by assessing fit. Total call length: 30 minutes. Include exact questions for each section and notes on what to listen for.
Discovery calls fail when coaches pitch too early. This structure ensures the client articulates their own pain — making them far more likely to self-qualify for your program.
Prompt 2
Write a client intake questionnaire for my coaching program. Program focus: [describe]. Ideal client: [describe]. I need to understand before our first session: their current situation, what they want to achieve, any previous attempts, how they prefer to communicate, and what success looks like in 90 days. Create 10 questions — mix of short answer and open-ended. Avoid yes/no questions. Include one question that reveals how coachable they are (not asked directly).
Great intake questionnaires do two things: help you prepare, and help the client get clear before they've even started. The "coachability" question is the most important thing most coaches never ask directly.
Prompt 3
Write a welcome email for a new coaching client who just signed their contract. Program: [name and brief description]. Start date: [date]. First session date: [date]. Include: (1) genuine congratulations that feels earned, not generic, (2) what to do before the first session (complete intake form, read [X], prepare [Y]), (3) how we'll communicate between sessions, (4) what makes this program different and why it works. Under 300 words. Warm but professional. From: [your name].
The first 48 hours of a coaching engagement set the tone for the entire relationship. A welcome email that shows you're prepared and specific to them reduces first-session anxiety and increases early commitment.
Prompt 4
Write a client agreement summary email (not the legal document — a plain English companion to it). Key terms to explain clearly: payment schedule ($[X] on [dates]), what happens if they need to reschedule (policy: [describe]), how confidentiality works, and what happens if they decide to stop early. Tone: conversational but professional. Goal: make them feel safe signing, not nervous. Under 250 words. This goes WITH the formal contract, not instead of it.
Clients who understand what they're signing become easier to work with. This companion email reduces "wait, what did I agree to?" conversations mid-program — and positions you as someone who prioritizes clarity over legalese.
Prompt 5
Write a first-session agenda for a new coaching client. Their stated goal: [describe from intake form]. Their primary challenge: [describe]. What I know about their situation: [any other intake details]. Session length: [60 or 90 minutes]. Structure: opening check-in (5 min), goal-setting and success metrics (20 min), understanding the current situation deeply (20 min), identifying the first key obstacle (10 min), action items for Week 1 (5 min), close. Include the specific questions I'll ask in each section.
First sessions either build momentum or create confusion. A structured first session that ends with clear Week 1 actions gives clients early wins — and clients with early wins become advocates.

📋Session Prep & Program Delivery (Prompts 6–10)

Prompt 6
Write a session recap and action item email for a coaching session I just completed. What we covered: [describe the main topics discussed]. Key insights the client had: [describe any breakthroughs or realizations]. Action items agreed: [list the specific actions, owners, and deadlines]. Next session: [date and topic]. Under 200 words. Keep it concise — this should take them 90 seconds to read and leave them motivated, not overwhelmed.
Post-session recaps are the single most underused tool in coaching. They lock in commitments, serve as accountability artifacts, and demonstrate your value as a structured thinker.
Prompt 7
My client is stuck on: [describe the exact situation — something they want to change but keeps avoiding, a decision they can't make, a habit they can't break]. They've tried: [describe what hasn't worked]. Create 5 coaching questions I can use in our next session to help them get unstuck — without me giving them the answer. Questions should progressively deepen: start with their current perspective, move to underlying assumptions, then toward what a future version of themselves would decide. Each question on its own line.
Coaches who ask better questions get faster client breakthroughs. This prompt generates the kind of questions that provoke genuine reflection, not just "good points" that don't lead to change.
Prompt 8
Write a mid-program check-in survey to send to a client at the halfway point of our [X]-month engagement. Goal: honestly assess whether they're getting value, surface any unspoken concerns, and reinforce their progress. Include: (1) a 1-10 overall satisfaction question with follow-up, (2) what's been most valuable, (3) what could be better, (4) whether they're completing their action items (honestly), (5) what they most want to focus on in the second half. 5-7 questions total. Conversational tone — not a corporate survey.
Mid-program check-ins catch problems before they become refund requests. A client who tells you they're confused at week 4 is a client you can save. One who doesn't tell you until week 8 is a client you've already lost.
Prompt 9
Create a 90-day progress report template for a coaching client nearing the end of their program. Sections: (1) Original goal vs. where they are now, (2) Key milestones achieved (fill in during session), (3) Specific behaviors or mindset shifts that changed, (4) Remaining gaps and recommendations, (5) Metrics: [list any numbers that matter — revenue, team size, time saved, habits formed], (6) Recommended next steps (upsell or referral pathway). Professional but warm. Should be something the client would keep and share.
A well-written 90-day progress report does three things: validates the client's investment, creates a document they share with others (free marketing), and opens the conversation about continued work.
Prompt 10
A client just told me they're not seeing the results they expected halfway through the program. Their concern: [describe exactly what they said]. What I know from our sessions: [describe the real situation — are they following through on actions, what's actually blocking progress?]. Write a response email that: acknowledges their concern genuinely, offers a specific diagnosis of what's happening (not blame), proposes a concrete adjustment to the program, and reinforces your commitment to their outcome. Under 250 words. Do not be defensive.
How you handle a "this isn't working" conversation determines whether you get a refund request or a testimonial. This prompt forces a non-defensive, solutions-first response that shows leadership.

💼Proposals & Pricing (Prompts 11–15)

Prompt 11
Write a coaching proposal for a potential client. Client situation: [describe what you learned on the discovery call — their goal, their challenge, their timeline]. Proposed program: [describe: duration, format, number of sessions, any deliverables]. Investment: $[X]. Structure the proposal as: (1) What you heard them say about their goal, (2) Why your approach is the right fit for their situation, (3) What the program includes (specifically, not generically), (4) Expected outcomes in their language, (5) Investment and what it includes, (6) Simple next step. Under 400 words. No jargon. No bullet-point filler.
Proposals fail when they're about the coach. This structure mirrors back what the client said in discovery — making them feel deeply understood before you ask for the investment.
Prompt 12
A potential client pushed back on my coaching fee of $[X]. They said: "[paste their exact words]." Write 3 different responses for 3 different scenarios: (1) they genuinely can't afford it but are a strong fit, (2) they're testing if you'll discount (but can afford it), (3) they need to justify ROI to themselves or a partner. For each scenario, write a response that holds the price but addresses the real concern. Do not discount in scenarios 2 or 3 without getting something in return.
Price negotiation responses must be calibrated to the actual objection. Discounting when someone is just testing you trains them to push back on everything. This prompt forces you to diagnose before you respond.
Prompt 13
Write the copy for three coaching package options following a good-better-best structure. My coaching focus: [describe]. My core 3-month program is $[X]. Design packages around it: (1) a lighter "starter" option at ~60% of the price (fewer sessions, some async support), (2) the core program, (3) a premium "accelerator" at ~150% of the price (more sessions, direct access, bonus deliverable). For each package: write the name, tagline, what's included in plain English, and who it's for. Make the middle option the obvious choice.
Tiered pricing anchors clients to the middle option and eliminates "yes or no" decisions. Coaches who offer only one package often lose clients who wanted more — or couldn't afford full price.
Prompt 14
Write a referral request email to a current or recently graduated client. What they've achieved: [specific result in their words if possible — e.g., "went from 3 clients to 9 in 4 months," "got promoted," "launched their first group program"]. My ask: a specific referral to someone they know who might be dealing with [describe the starting problem you solve]. Keep it short (under 150 words). Don't use the word "referral" — make it feel like a personal ask, not a business transaction. Offer nothing in return — reciprocity without payment preserves relationship quality.
The best time to ask for a referral is immediately after a win. This email capitalizes on the moment when a client is most proud of their progress — and most likely to want their friends to have the same experience.
Prompt 15
Write a re-engagement email to a past client I haven't spoken to in [X] months. What they worked on with me: [brief description]. What I know about where they might be now: [any updates — did they achieve the goal? What's the likely next challenge?]. Goal: restart the conversation naturally — not to pitch immediately. Write it as a genuine check-in that gives a small piece of value (insight, relevant resource, or congratulations on something) and leaves the door open. Under 100 words.
Past clients are 5x easier to close than new prospects. A well-timed "thinking of you" email — especially one tied to where they likely are now — converts at remarkably high rates without any hard pitch.

📢Content Creation & Marketing (Prompts 16–20)

Prompt 16
Write a LinkedIn post about a client transformation story (anonymized). The journey: they came to me with [starting problem]. The shift that changed everything: [describe the key insight or action that unlocked progress]. Where they are now: [result]. Format: 3-4 short paragraphs, conversational, no hashtag spam (2-3 max). End with a question that invites readers to reflect on their own situation. Do not make it an ad. It should read like a genuine observation, not a case study header.
Transformation stories on LinkedIn outperform any promotional content because they let the reader project themselves into the story. This format generates DMs from people who recognize themselves in the "before."
Prompt 17
Write a weekly email newsletter for my coaching audience. Topic this week: [describe a common challenge your ideal client faces]. Structure: (1) a short story or observation that opens the topic (under 80 words), (2) the main insight or reframe (your perspective, 150-200 words), (3) one practical thing they can try this week (specific, not vague), (4) a brief connection to your coaching work (soft, optional). Total length: 350-450 words. Conversational tone — sounds like it's from a person, not a company. From: [your name].
Weekly newsletters that teach specific things build more trust than monthly newsletters that recap achievements. The "one thing to try" section is what converts readers into clients — it shows you understand implementation, not just theory.
Prompt 18
Write a testimonial request email for a coaching client who recently got a strong result. Their result: [describe]. How to ask: request a short testimonial (3-5 sentences) addressing three things: (1) what situation brought them to coaching, (2) what changed during our work together, (3) what result they can point to now. Give them the option to write it themselves OR answer 3 questions and have me write it from their answers. Keep the email under 100 words and make it easy to say yes in 5 minutes.
Giving clients two options (write it or answer questions) dramatically increases response rates. The "answer 3 questions" path gets better testimonials because you control what they speak to — and clients love having someone else do the writing.
Prompt 19
I want to write about: [coaching topic or framework you know deeply]. Create an outline for a long-form piece (blog post, LinkedIn article, or newsletter) that is genuinely useful to [ideal client type] — not a pitch for my services. Structure: a strong hook that names a specific, relatable struggle, 3-4 practical insights with examples or stories for each, a closing section that reframes how they think about the problem, and a natural soft mention of what I do (optional, last). Length target: 800-1000 words. The piece should be shareable on its own merits.
Long-form content that ranks organically and gets shared on LinkedIn generates inbound leads without direct outreach. The "soft mention at the end" rule ensures you build authority first — which makes the mention far more effective than leading with it.
Prompt 20
Write the sales page copy for my coaching program. Program: [name and brief description]. Ideal client: [who they are, what situation they're in]. Transformation: [what changes for them — be specific, use numbers if you have them]. Structure the page: (1) headline that names the result + who it's for, (2) the problem in their language (not your language), (3) why most attempts at this goal fail, (4) how your approach is different, (5) what's included (specifics), (6) about you (short, credibility-focused), (7) testimonials (leave placeholder), (8) FAQ (3 real objections, answered honestly), (9) CTA with price. This is for a real sales page, not a brochure.
Most coaching sales pages fail because they describe the program, not the transformation. This structure forces you to write about the client's experience — which is what creates the "this is for me" feeling that drives sign-ups.
