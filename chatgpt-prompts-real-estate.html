🏠Property Listing Descriptions (Prompts 1–5)

Prompt 1
Write a compelling MLS listing description for this property: [address or description]. Key features: [list 5-8 features]. Target buyer: [first-time buyers / move-up buyers / investors / retirees]. Tone: [warm and inviting / modern and sleek / classic and prestigious]. Length: 150-200 words. Lead with the strongest lifestyle benefit, not a room count.
Most listing descriptions start with "Stunning 3BR/2BA home" — generic, forgettable. This prompt forces a lifestyle-first approach that stops scrollers.
Prompt 2
Rewrite this property description to be more compelling: [paste current description]. Keep all the facts but improve: (1) the opening hook, (2) how we describe the neighborhood lifestyle, (3) the closing call to action. Do not add features that aren't in the original.
Perfect for reviving stale listings. Keeps accuracy while dramatically improving engagement.
Prompt 3
Write 3 different listing headline options for this property: [describe the property's best feature — e.g., "corner lot with mountain views," "renovated kitchen in top school district," "investor-ready duplex"]. Each headline should be under 12 words and lead with the benefit, not the feature.
Headlines are what get clicked in Zillow/Realtor.com search results. Three options gives you testing flexibility.
Prompt 4
Write a social media caption (under 150 words) for this new listing: [property address or brief description]. Include: one attention-grabbing opening line, 3 key selling points, price, and a clear call to action (DM me / link in bio / schedule a showing). Add 10 relevant hashtags at the end.
Combines listing announcement + social post in one prompt. Works for Instagram, Facebook, and LinkedIn with minor tweaks.
Prompt 5
This property has been on the market for [X] days without an offer. Write a price reduction announcement for: (1) MLS remarks update (100 words), (2) email to my buyer list, (3) social media post. Tone: create urgency without making it sound desperate. Current price: $[X]. New price: $[X]. Key reasons it's now the best value in [neighborhood]: [list 2-3 reasons].
Price reductions are awkward to communicate. This framing turns a negative (sitting on market) into a buying opportunity narrative.

📧Buyer & Seller Emails (Prompts 6–10)

Prompt 6
Write a first response email to a new buyer inquiry who asked about [property address]. They haven't signed a buyer agreement yet. Goal: (1) answer their specific question about [their question], (2) qualify their situation without being intrusive, (3) propose a next step (showing or phone call). Keep it under 150 words. Sound like a person, not a template.
First response speed and quality determine if a lead converts. This balances helpfulness with lead qualification naturally.
Prompt 7
Write a monthly market update email for my buyer database. Market conditions: [describe current market — e.g., "inventory is tight, prices up 8% YoY, rates at X%"]. My area: [city/neighborhood]. Length: 200-250 words. Include: a short market summary in plain English, what it means for buyers right now, and one property I'm currently showing (link placeholder). Tone: trusted advisor, not sales pitch.
Monthly market emails keep you top-of-mind with dormant buyers. This format positions you as the expert, not just an agent fishing for business.
Prompt 8
Write a follow-up email to a seller whose listing has expired without selling. My name: [name]. Their property: [address]. Their situation: they were frustrated by [low showings / price / marketing / competing listings]. Opening goal: acknowledge their frustration without criticizing the previous agent. Main message: here's what I would do differently. CTA: 15-minute call, no obligation. Under 200 words.
Expired listing outreach is one of the highest-ROI prospecting activities. This approach leads with empathy, not self-promotion.
Prompt 9
Write a "we're in contract" email to a seller client. Property: [address]. Key details: offer price $[X], closing date [date], key contingencies [list]. Tone: celebratory but professional. Include: what happens next (inspection period, appraisal, title), what the seller needs to do (nothing / start packing / arrange utilities), and my direct contact if questions arise. Under 250 words.
Sellers are anxious during the contract period. A clear, calm "what happens next" email prevents panic calls and builds trust.
Prompt 10
Write a post-closing thank you email to a buyer client. Their names: [names]. Property: [address]. Closing date: [date]. Add a personal touch: [one thing you remember about their search — e.g., "found the pantry they were holding out for," "closed 2 weeks faster than expected"]. Include: a request for a Google review (but make it feel genuine, not transactional), and an offer to help their friends/family. Under 150 words.
The post-close period is when referrals are born. A warm, personalized email at this moment dramatically increases review and referral rates.

📱Social Media Content (Prompts 11–15)

Prompt 11
Write a "just sold" Instagram post for: [address or brief description, sale price, days on market]. Include: a short story about the sale (without sharing private client info), what made this deal work, and a hook for potential sellers. End with a CTA. Tone: proud but humble. Add 10 relevant hashtags. Keep it under 200 words.
"Just sold" posts are your social proof. Framing them as mini case studies (here's what worked) is more compelling than "Another one sold!"
Prompt 12
Write a LinkedIn post about a lesson I learned from a recent real estate transaction: [describe the situation — a deal that almost fell through, a negotiation win, a buyer who waited too long, etc.]. Format: 3-4 short paragraphs, conversational tone, end with a question for engagement. No hashtags on LinkedIn — this is a professional insight post, not a listing announcement.
LinkedIn rewards expertise content. Agents who share real lessons get far more engagement than those who just post listings.
Prompt 13
Write 5 educational carousel slide captions for Instagram on the topic: "[topic — e.g., first-time buyer mistakes, how to win in a multiple offer situation, what to fix before listing]." Each slide should have: a bold headline (under 8 words), 2-3 sentences of explanation, and a "swipe for more" or "save this" CTA on slides 2-4. Final slide: CTA to DM me or visit my profile.
Carousel posts get 3x more engagement on Instagram than single-image posts. Educational content builds your expert positioning while reaching new audiences.
Prompt 14
Write a monthly neighborhood spotlight post (Facebook or Instagram). Neighborhood: [name]. Include: 3 things locals love about this area, 1 hidden gem (restaurant, park, shop, etc.), current market snapshot in 1-2 sentences (median price, avg days on market), and a question for the community. Under 200 words. Tone: local expert who genuinely loves the area.
Neighborhood content positions you as the local authority — the first agent people think of when they want to buy or sell in that area.
Prompt 15
Write a real estate myth-busting post for social media. Myth: "[common buyer or seller misconception — e.g., 'You need 20% down to buy a home,' 'Spring is always the best time to sell,' 'You should price high to leave room to negotiate']." Format: (1) State the myth, (2) The reality in 2-3 sentences with a specific fact or stat, (3) What this means for buyers/sellers today, (4) CTA. Under 175 words.
Myth-busting content earns shares and saves because it directly addresses things people believe but shouldn't. High educational value, high reach.

🤝Negotiation & Objection Handling (Prompts 16–20)

Prompt 16
Help me draft a counter-offer strategy email to my seller client. Situation: buyer offered $[X] on a home listed at $[Y]. The offer includes [list contingencies]. My recommendation to the seller is to [counter at $X / accept / reject] because [reason]. Write this as a clear, logical explanation my seller can understand, with the emotional reassurance they need to make a confident decision. Under 200 words.
Sellers make emotional decisions at the counter-offer stage. This frames your recommendation as clear logic, reducing second-guessing.
Prompt 17
Write a response to this seller objection: "I want to list higher than your CMA suggests because my neighbor's house sold for more." My actual recommendation is $[X]. The neighbor's sale was at $[Y] and it differs because [list 2-3 factual differences — e.g., more square footage, renovated kitchen, different street]. Write a confident but respectful response that acknowledges their feelings, explains the difference factually, and maintains my pricing recommendation. Under 150 words.
The overpriced listing conversation is one of the hardest in real estate. This gives you language that respects the client while holding your professional ground.
Prompt 18
A buyer client just asked "why should I use you instead of going directly to the listing agent?" Write me a 90-second verbal response (formatted as spoken word, natural tone) that explains the value of buyer representation — specifically: fiduciary duty, negotiation expertise, access to off-market info, and the fact that my commission is typically paid by the seller. Do not make it sound rehearsed or defensive.
Every buyer agent needs a crisp, honest answer to this. This prompt produces a natural-sounding response you can internalize and use in the conversation.
Prompt 19
Write a response to a seller who says "I want to try selling it myself (FSBO) first." Acknowledge their goal (saving commission). Explain — without being condescending — the 3 main challenges FSBO sellers encounter: pricing accuracy, buyer agent cooperation, and contract/legal complexity. Offer a specific compromise: [e.g., list with me and I'll reduce my fee if you find your own buyer, OR I'll give you a free CMA and check back in 30 days]. Keep it conversational, under 175 words.
FSBO objections require patience and respect. This approach plants a seed without pressure — many FSBO sellers come back to agents within 60 days.
Prompt 20
After a home inspection, the buyer is requesting $[X] in repairs or credits on a property priced at $[Y]. Write two versions of my seller's counter-response: (1) Seller agrees to $[lower amount] in credit but no repairs, with rationale, (2) Seller declines all requests with rationale. For each: write the actual counter language AND the email I send my seller client explaining why I'm recommending this approach. Keep each version under 200 words.
Post-inspection negotiations are where deals die. Having both versions ready lets you move fast and advise your client confidently.
Pro tip: For best results, always give ChatGPT context about your local market, your personal style, and the specific client situation. The more specific your input, the more on-target the output. Replace every [BRACKET] before sending.
