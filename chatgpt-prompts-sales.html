📩Cold Outreach (Prompts 1–5)

Prompt 1
Write a cold outreach email to [prospect's job title] at [company type/industry]. My product/service: [describe in one sentence]. Their likely pain point: [describe the problem I solve]. Email goal: get a 15-minute call, not close the deal. Keep it under 100 words. No subject line fluff. Start with their problem, not my product. End with one easy yes/no question.
Cold emails fail when they open with "I'm reaching out because..." — that's about you. This forces a problem-first opening that makes the prospect feel understood before you've said anything about yourself.
Prompt 2
Write 3 different subject lines for a cold email targeting [prospect type] about [value proposition]. Each subject line should use a different approach: (1) curiosity/question, (2) specific result or number, (3) their name + direct benefit. Keep each under 50 characters. No clickbait. Each should accurately reflect the email body.
Subject line is 80% of open rate. Three variants gives you the ability to A/B test — and the number-based option typically outperforms by 20-30% in B2B cold email.
Prompt 3
I want to reach out to [prospect name] at [company]. Before emailing, I researched: [paste 2-3 things you found — recent news, LinkedIn post, job change, product launch, etc.]. Write a cold email that references this research naturally (not in a stalker way). Show that I did my homework without making the email about me showing off. Goal: 15-min call. Under 120 words.
Personalized cold emails get 6x higher reply rates than generic ones. This prompt turns 5 minutes of LinkedIn research into an email that feels like you've been following their work for months.
Prompt 4
Write a cold LinkedIn connection request message for [prospect type]. I'm reaching out because [specific reason — mutual connection, their recent post, company news, etc.]. Keep it under 300 characters (LinkedIn limit for notes). Don't pitch. Just give a genuine reason to connect and make it easy to say yes.
LinkedIn connection requests that mention something specific to the person have 40% higher acceptance rates. This prompt forces specificity and removes the temptation to pitch before connecting.
Prompt 5
Write a referral outreach email using this context: I was referred to [prospect name] by [mutual contact]. The mutual contact said [brief context — what they mentioned about the prospect's needs]. My offering: [describe]. Keep it conversational — mention the mutual contact naturally in the first sentence, don't delay it. Under 100 words. End with a specific ask (15-min call this week or next).
Referral emails convert at 3-5x the rate of cold emails — but most reps write them like cold emails. Leading with the mutual contact's name in sentence one is the highest-leverage sentence in sales.

🔄Follow-Up Sequences (Prompts 6–10)

Prompt 6
Write a follow-up email for a prospect who didn't reply to my first outreach. Original email sent: [X] days ago about [topic]. New angle: instead of repeating the pitch, add a piece of value — [a relevant stat, case study snippet, or one useful insight related to their industry]. Keep it under 80 words. End with a different, even easier ask than the first email (e.g., "just reply with a thumbs up if you'd like to see more").
80% of sales happen after the 5th follow-up, but most reps stop at 2. The key is adding value each time, not just bumping the thread. This prompt forces a value-first approach to follow-up #2.
Prompt 7
Write a "break-up" follow-up email — the last message in a sequence to a prospect who hasn't responded after [X] attempts. Tone: genuinely kind, zero resentment, leaves the door open forever. Include: (1) an acknowledgment that the timing may not be right, (2) a one-line reminder of what I do and why it might matter to them later, (3) an easy opt-out that still feels warm. Under 75 words.
Break-up emails have the highest reply rates in any sequence because they create urgency without pressure. A well-written break-up email often generates replies from people who ignored 5 previous attempts.
Prompt 8
Write a follow-up email after a discovery call that went well. The prospect seemed interested in [specific aspect of your solution]. Their biggest concern was [objection or question they raised]. Next step agreed: [what was agreed — send proposal, schedule demo, follow up in 2 weeks, etc.]. Email goals: recap the call, show you listened, confirm the next step. Under 150 words. Include a 2-3 bullet summary of what we discussed.
Post-call follow-ups that include a concise summary have 40% higher next-step completion rates. They also protect you — if the prospect "misremembers" the agreed next step, this email is your record.
Prompt 9
Write a check-in email to a prospect who went cold after a positive conversation 3-4 weeks ago. They had shown interest in [product/service] but stopped responding. New trigger: [something relevant that just happened — industry news, a competitor announcement, a relevant case study you can share]. Keep it short (under 80 words). Reopen the conversation naturally — don't reference that they went cold.
Ghosting after a good call is usually about timing, not interest. A well-timed re-engagement tied to a relevant trigger can restart a conversation that felt dead.
Prompt 10
Write a follow-up email to a prospect after sending a proposal [X] days ago with no response. Do NOT ask "did you get a chance to review it?" — that's weak. Instead: (1) assume they've read it, (2) address the most likely objection they're sitting on: [price / timing / ROI / needs sign-off from someone else], (3) offer a specific piece of reassurance or social proof for that objection. Under 100 words.
Proposal follow-ups fail when they re-ask instead of advance. By addressing the most likely unspoken objection proactively, you show you understand their situation and move the deal forward.

🔍Discovery & Qualification (Prompts 11–15)

Prompt 11
I have a discovery call with [prospect role] at [company type] in [X] minutes. Their industry: [industry]. What I know about them: [any research — LinkedIn, website, news]. Generate 8 discovery questions for this call using the SPIN selling framework (Situation, Problem, Implication, Need-Payoff). Prioritize questions that uncover the cost of their current problem, not just the problem itself. Include one unexpected question that most salespeople wouldn't ask.
SPIN questions close deals because they help prospects calculate the cost of inaction — before you ever mention your product. "What happens if this problem isn't solved by Q3?" is worth more than any feature list.
Prompt 12
Write a post-discovery call debrief I can complete in 5 minutes. Fields to fill: (1) main pain identified, (2) cost of the pain (what they said or implied), (3) decision-making process (who else is involved), (4) timeline, (5) budget signals, (6) biggest objection risk, (7) recommended next step, (8) probability this closes in 30/60/90 days. Format as a simple template I can reuse after every call.
Most reps rely on memory for call debrief. This structured capture makes your CRM notes actually useful — and forces you to assess deal probability honestly before momentum clouds your judgment.
Prompt 13
Write a company research brief for my discovery call with [company name]. Using what you know about them (or based on these notes: [paste anything you've found]), create a one-page briefing with: (1) company overview, (2) likely pain points given their industry and size, (3) 3 questions I should lead with, (4) potential red flags (budget, authority, need, timing), (5) competitor products they might already use. Keep it scannable — I need to read this in 5 minutes before the call.
Reps who do pre-call research close at 2x the rate of those who wing it. This prompt turns 5 minutes of research into a structured brief that makes you sound like you've worked their industry for years.
Prompt 14
A prospect just told me: "[paste their exact words describing their situation]." Based on what they said: (1) identify the core pain they're describing, (2) identify what they didn't say but probably feel, (3) suggest the best follow-up question to go deeper, (4) flag any red flags in what they said (is this real pain or a surface-level complaint?). Use this to help me respond thoughtfully in the next 30 seconds of the call.
Great salespeople hear what prospects mean, not just what they say. This prompt helps you process complex statements in real-time and find the "and what else?" question that unlocks the real conversation.
Prompt 15
Write a qualification scorecard for evaluating leads in my sales pipeline. My ideal customer: [describe — company size, industry, role, situation]. Rate each prospect 1-5 on: budget (can they afford it?), authority (am I talking to the decision-maker?), need (do they have an urgent problem I solve?), timeline (are they buying in the next 90 days?). Include specific questions to uncover each factor and a scoring guide: what score means pursue, what score means nurture, what score means disqualify.
BANT qualification prevents you from spending 3 months on deals that were never going to close. A simple scorecard surfaces bad fits early — so you can spend more time on deals that have real potential.

🤝Closing & Objection Handling (Prompts 16–20)

Prompt 16
A prospect said: "Your price is too high." Write 3 different responses for different contexts: (1) when price is a genuine budget issue, (2) when price is an objection but they have budget (they just need to justify the ROI), (3) when the objection is really about uncertain value (they're not sure the outcome is worth it). For each context, write a response that doesn't immediately discount — instead, reframes the conversation around value, ROI, or risk.
"Your price is too high" is almost never about price. It's about uncertain value. Three responses for three underlying reasons means you can choose the right one based on what you actually learned in discovery.
Prompt 17
A prospect said: "I need to think about it." Write a response that: (1) acknowledges this genuinely without being dismissive, (2) surfaces the real hesitation without being pushy, (3) creates a natural path to a specific next decision point (not just "call me when you're ready"). Keep it conversational — this is a verbal response, not an email. Under 80 words.
"I need to think about it" is the most common stall in sales. Reps who say "of course, take your time!" lose the deal. This response uncovers what's really holding them back without making them feel interrogated.
Prompt 18
Write a ROI/business case email I can send to a prospect who needs to get internal buy-in. Their situation: they personally want to move forward but need to convince [their boss / procurement / finance / a committee]. My product: [describe]. Key result it delivers: [describe in measurable terms — time saved, revenue increased, risk reduced]. Write a one-page business case they can forward internally. Include: problem statement, solution summary, ROI calculation (template with placeholders for their numbers), and risks of NOT acting.
B2B deals die in "committee." Giving your champion a ready-made business case they can forward internally turns them into an internal sales rep — the most powerful person in any deal.
Prompt 19
A prospect said: "We're going with a competitor." I know: (1) the competitor they chose: [name], (2) why they chose them (based on the conversation): [reason — price, feature, relationship, incumbent, etc.]. Write a graceful response that: keeps the door open for future business, asks one respectful question to learn from the loss, and leaves them with a positive impression of me. Under 80 words. This is not a last-ditch pitch — it's relationship management.
30% of "lost" deals come back within 18 months when the winning vendor disappoints. The salespeople who stay respectful in the loss call are the ones those prospects call when they're unhappy later.
Prompt 20
Write a closing email for a deal that's been in late stages for [X] weeks without moving. The prospect has expressed genuine interest but keeps pushing the decision. Context: [what they've said about the delay]. Create a "now or never" email that creates urgency without being manipulative. The urgency must be real (not fake "this offer expires Friday"). Real reasons could include: [upcoming price increase / end of quarter / limited onboarding slots / relevant external deadline]. Under 120 words.
Fake urgency destroys trust. Real urgency closes deals. This prompt forces you to find the genuine deadline — which sharpens your close without making the prospect feel gamed.
Pro tip: These prompts work best when you paste in real context — actual prospect quotes, real company names, specific numbers. The more specific the input, the more persuasive the output. Generic inputs produce generic emails.
