📝Blog & Content Writing (Prompts 1–8)

Prompt 1
Turn a topic into a complete blog outline
Create a detailed blog post outline for "[TOPIC]" targeting the keyword "[PRIMARY KEYWORD]". The post should be [WORD COUNT] words. Include: an SEO-optimized H1, 6-8 H2 sections with 2-3 H3 subsections each, a FAQ section based on questions readers likely ask, and a conclusion with a CTA. Target audience: [AUDIENCE DESCRIPTION]. Mark which sections need statistics or expert quotes.
Why it works: Starting with structure kills blank-page paralysis. The outline is the hardest part — once it exists, writing fills in like water.
Prompt 2
Write an introduction that doesn't start with "In today's..."
Write the introduction paragraph (150-200 words) for a blog post titled "[TITLE]". Open with a surprising statistic or a specific relatable scenario — not a generic observation. Establish the reader's problem in 2-3 sentences, promise what they'll learn, and naturally include the keyword "[PRIMARY KEYWORD]" within the first 100 words. Do NOT start with "In today's...", "Are you looking for...", or "Have you ever..."
Why it works: The first sentence determines whether the rest gets read. A generic opener signals a generic post.
Prompt 3
Write 5 hook styles for the same post
Write 5 different opening hooks for a blog post about [TOPIC]: 1. Statistic hook — open with one surprising, specific number 2. Story hook — a first-person anecdote that mirrors the reader's exact situation 3. Question hook — the specific question they're Googling right now 4. Contrarian hook — challenge a belief most people in [AUDIENCE] hold 5. Future-pacing hook — describe their life 30 days after solving the problem Rate each 1-10 for likely engagement with [AUDIENCE].
Why it works: Testing 5 hooks takes 2 minutes and almost always reveals one that's clearly better than the others.
Prompt 4
Generate a month of blog post ideas
Generate 20 blog post title ideas for a content strategy targeting [NICHE AUDIENCE] interested in [BROAD TOPIC]. For each title: estimated search intent (informational/commercial/transactional), difficulty level (low/medium/competitive), monetization potential, and suggested word count. Mix formats: how-to, listicle, comparison, ultimate guide, personal experience. Flag the 5 with the highest potential for organic traffic.
Why it works: Content calendar creation is one of the highest-leverage uses of AI — planning 20 posts in 5 minutes instead of 2 hours.
Prompt 5
Write a "how to" section for any tutorial
Write a "how to" section for [SPECIFIC TASK] within a blog post about [BROADER TOPIC]. Structure: 1-sentence intro, 5-7 numbered steps (each 1-2 sentences), a "common mistake" callout, and a "pro tip" box. Make the steps specific enough that [BEGINNER AUDIENCE] could follow without clicking away for more information. Assume they know nothing about [PREREQUISITE KNOWLEDGE].
Why it works: Tutorial sections are where writers stall — the step-by-step logic is where the blank page hits hardest.
Prompt 6
Write 3 endings for the same post
Write 3 different conclusions for a blog post about [TOPIC] targeting [AUDIENCE]: 1. Summary — recap the 3 key points + a forward-looking statement 2. Challenge — issue one specific action they can take in the next 24 hours 3. Question — end with something that genuinely makes them think and invites a comment For each: include a natural CTA leading to [YOUR NEXT ACTION].
Why it works: The ending converts browsers into subscribers or buyers. Three options let you pick the one that matches your post's energy.
Prompt 7
Turn any section into a scannable listicle
Rewrite this section of my blog post as a scannable listicle: [PASTE YOUR DRAFT SECTION]. Keep the same information but format it as: bold H3 for each point, 2-3 sentence explanation, one specific example or stat, and a practical takeaway in a callout box. Add subheadings and bold key terms so a reader skimming at 30 seconds/page gets the main idea.
Why it works: 80% of readers scan before reading. Scannable formatting is the difference between a "bounce" and an engaged read.
Prompt 8
Write meta description + title tag variations
Write 5 title tag and meta description pairs for a blog post about [TOPIC] targeting the keyword "[PRIMARY KEYWORD]". Each title: under 60 characters, front-loaded keyword, unique angle. Each meta description: under 155 characters, includes the keyword, ends with an implicit CTA. Rate each pair for likely click-through rate. Flag which would cause Google to rewrite your title.
Why it works: SEO writers lose 20 minutes per post to this. AI does it in 30 seconds.

📖Fiction & Storytelling (Prompts 9–16)

Prompt 9
Build a character that feels like a real person
Create a fully developed character profile for [CHARACTER NAME/TYPE] in a [GENRE] story. Include: a specific physical description (avoid generic), one defining childhood moment + one recent pivotal event, their core desire (what they most want), their core fear (what drives every decision), their fatal flaw (what might destroy them), a 50-word voice sample in their dialogue, and 3 quirks or habits that make them recognizable. No stock character tropes.
Why it works: Flat characters are the most common reason readers stop reading. This structure forces specificity.
Prompt 10
Make a generic protagonist compelling
My protagonist is [DESCRIPTION]. They feel generic. Help me make them specific. Give me: 5 details only this character would have (not standard for this genre), a personality contradiction (two traits that shouldn't go together but do in this person), the most embarrassing moment from their past that still affects them today, and how they'd react to [SPECIFIC SITUATION] in a way unique to their character. Current description: [PASTE YOUR CURRENT DESCRIPTION].
Why it works: Generic protagonists come from generic questions. Specific questions produce specific characters.
Prompt 11
Plot a story beat-by-beat
I have this story premise: [YOUR PREMISE]. Help me develop it using the Save the Cat beat sheet. Walk through all 15 beats specifically for my story: Opening Image, Theme Stated, Set-Up, Catalyst, Debate, Break into Two, B Story, Fun and Games, Midpoint, Bad Guys Close In, All Is Lost, Dark Night of the Soul, Break into Three, Finale, Final Image. Make each beat specific to my story — no generic placeholder advice.
Why it works: Beat sheets are the fastest way to find structural holes before you've written 30,000 words into a dead end.
Prompt 12
Write a scene you're dreading
Help me write a [TYPE OF SCENE: confrontation / emotional breakdown / action sequence / revelation] between [CHARACTER A] and [CHARACTER B]. Context: [1-2 sentence setup]. What each character wants from this scene: [A wants X / B wants Y]. The emotional shift that must happen by the end: [DESCRIBE IT]. Start the scene in the middle of the action, not with setup. Show conflict through subtext first, then escalate. 400-600 words.
Why it works: Writers avoid the scenes they dread writing — which are usually the most important scenes. This prompt gets you over the wall.
Prompt 13
Find the universal truth in a personal story
I'm writing a personal essay about [PERSONAL EXPERIENCE/LESSON]. My experience: [DESCRIBE IT]. The lesson I took from it: [YOUR LESSON]. Help me find the universal truth — the version of this story that any reader could recognize in their own life. Rewrite the opening: start with the specific moment, expand to the universal, set up the rest of the piece. Under 200 words. Make the reader think "this is my story too."
Why it works: Personal essays fail when they stay personal. The moment you find the universal, you have an audience.
Prompt 14
Write dialogue that sounds human
Rewrite this dialogue to sound more natural: [PASTE YOUR DIALOGUE]. Problems to fix: people don't speak in complete sentences, they interrupt, they avoid the real subject, they say things that mean something different than the words. Rewrite so that: each character has a distinct voice, the real conflict is communicated through subtext, not statement, and it advances the scene without exposition dumps. [SCENE CONTEXT: brief summary].
Why it works: Stiff dialogue is the most common "writer's tell." Natural dialogue is the hardest thing to teach and the easiest thing for AI to model once you describe the characters.
Prompt 15
Build a world from one detail
I'm building a [GENRE] world. One detail I know: [YOUR DETAIL — e.g., "there's no spoken language", "magic costs years off your life", "everyone can see 3 minutes into the future"]. Extrapolate from this single detail: what are the 5 most significant implications for society, relationships, government, and daily life? What creates the conflict? What does this world's most ordinary Tuesday look like for a regular person? What can't exist in this world that exists in ours?
Why it works: World-building paralysis is real. A single well-chosen detail, rigorously extrapolated, is better than 50 inconsistent ideas.
Prompt 16
Get unstuck when your plot stops making sense
I'm stuck in my story. Here's where I am: [DESCRIBE THE SITUATION]. My protagonist needs to get from [POINT A] to [POINT B] but every path I've tried feels contrived or boring. Give me 5 different ways to get them there — each one using a different technique: coincidence (then explain how to make it not feel like coincidence), character decision driven by flaw, external force, reversal of what the character thinks they want, and escalating consequence of an earlier choice. I'll pick one.
Why it works: Plot problems aren't about imagination — they're about having options. This prompt generates options fast.
Tip: Chain prompts together. Use Prompt 11 (beat sheet) to plan, Prompt 9 (character) to build your people, then Prompt 12 (scene) to write the scenes you're dreading. Each output feeds the next.

✍️Copywriting (Prompts 17–22)

Prompt 17
Write a landing page that converts
Write a landing page for [PRODUCT/SERVICE] using the PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solution) framework. Audience: [DESCRIBE THEM]. The specific problem they have: [DESCRIBE IT PRECISELY]. Agitation: [WHAT MAKES THE PROBLEM WORSE / WHY IT'S URGENT]. My solution and why it's different: [DESCRIBE]. Include: headline + subheadline, 3-bullet benefit block (outcomes, not features), one-paragraph proof/credibility, FAQ addressing the top 2 objections, and a CTA button text. Under 400 words total.
Why it works: PAS is the most reliable copywriting framework because it starts where the reader already is — in their problem.
Prompt 18
Write 10 headline variations
Write 10 headline variations for [PRODUCT/SERVICE/OFFER]. The core benefit: [DESCRIBE IT]. Target reader: [DESCRIBE THEM]. Use these 10 formulas: 1. The Direct Benefit headline 2. How To + big promise 3. Specific number + specific outcome 4. "The [ADJECTIVE] way to [OUTCOME]" 5. Question that creates urgency 6. Before/After (2 states) 7. Contrarian ("Why [conventional approach] doesn't work") 8. The Secret/Little-Known 9. Time-bound ("In [X] minutes/days/weeks") 10. Social proof angle Rate each 1-10 for likely performance with this audience.
Why it works: Most copywriters write 3 headlines and pick. Top copywriters write 25. This prompt gets you to 10 in 60 seconds.
Prompt 19
Write a sales email that doesn't feel salesy
Write a sales email for [PRODUCT/SERVICE] at [PRICE POINT]. Audience: [DESCRIBE]. The email must: open with a story or observation (NOT "I'm reaching out because..."), name the specific problem clearly without catastrophizing, introduce the product as the natural next step (not a hard sell), include one concrete result or outcome, address the top objection in one sentence, end with a low-pressure CTA. Under 250 words. Subject line options (3).
Why it works: Sales emails that open with "I" get deleted. Emails that open with the reader's world get read.
Prompt 20
Turn features into benefits
I have a product with these features: [LIST YOUR FEATURES]. For each feature, translate it into: the direct benefit (what it does for the user), the emotional benefit (how it makes them feel), and the "so what?" — why this specific person cares. Then write a 2-sentence benefit bullet for each that a marketer would use in copy. Target customer: [DESCRIBE THEM]. Avoid starting bullets with "You will" or "This will."
Why it works: Features describe what a product is. Benefits describe why anyone should care. Most product copy stops at features.
Prompt 21
Handle a specific objection in copy
Write a copy block that handles this specific objection: "[PASTE THE OBJECTION — e.g., 'it's too expensive', 'I don't have time', 'I've tried this before']". Product/service: [DESCRIBE]. Approach: acknowledge the objection directly (don't dismiss it), reframe it with a concrete comparison or analogy, provide one specific proof point or example, and move back to the benefit without dwelling. Under 100 words. Tone: [honest/confident/empathetic].
Why it works: Objections left unaddressed become reasons not to buy. Named and handled objections become trust signals.
Prompt 22
Write product descriptions that sell
Write a product description for [PRODUCT] in [CATEGORY]. Format: 2-sentence hook (benefit-led, not feature-led), bullet list of 5-7 features formatted as benefits (outcome-focused), one sentence about who it's for, one sentence about what it's NOT for (creates specificity and trust), and a one-line CTA. Tone: [casual/professional/authoritative]. Competitor context if relevant: [DESCRIBE HOW COMPETITORS POSITION SIMILAR PRODUCTS]. Our differentiation: [DESCRIBE].
Why it works: Including "not for you if" builds more trust than a perfect sales pitch. It signals confidence and filters for the right buyer.

✂️Editing & Refinement (Prompts 23–27)

Prompt 23
Cut your word count by 30% without losing meaning
Tighten this passage by 30% without losing any meaning or changing my voice: [PASTE YOUR TEXT]. Rules: eliminate every word that doesn't carry weight, cut adverbs (replace with stronger verbs), cut hedging words ("somewhat", "quite", "rather", "a bit"), remove setup phrases ("I think that...", "It's important to note that..."), shorten prepositional chains. Show me the before/after word count and flag the 3 most common patterns you cut.
Why it works: 90% of first drafts are 30% too long. Shorter writing is almost always clearer and more persuasive.
Prompt 24
Fix passive voice without sounding robotic
Rewrite this passage in active voice: [PASTE YOUR TEXT]. Then: flag the 3 remaining places where passive voice would actually be better (when the actor is unknown, unimportant, or intentionally hidden). Explain the choice. Keep my voice — don't change any word that isn't passive construction. Mark every change so I can review and revert if needed.
Why it works: Passive voice rarely sounds intentional. Active voice is almost always stronger — but you want to understand which 10% to keep passive.
Prompt 25
Make your transitions invisible
Read these two sections of my piece and write 3 different transition options between them. [SECTION 1: PASTE] [SECTION 2: PASTE]. Transition option 1: a single sentence bridge. Option 2: a brief paragraph that uses a metaphor or analogy. Option 3: a question that makes the reader lean forward. Each transition should make the jump feel inevitable — the reader should not notice they've moved from one idea to the next.
Why it works: Jarring section breaks are invisible to writers who know the content, but immediately obvious to readers who don't.
Prompt 26
Find the buried lede
Read this piece and tell me: where is the most interesting sentence? [PASTE YOUR DRAFT]. The buried lede is usually the most specific detail, the most surprising reversal, or the most emotionally true moment — and writers bury it because they don't realize it's the hook. After finding it, rewrite the opening 150 words of the piece so it leads with that sentence or idea.
Why it works: Writers bury their best material in paragraph 4 because they're warming up. This prompt finds it fast.
Prompt 27
Hear your writing read back to you
Read this passage and give me a reader's honest reaction — not an editor's critique: [PASTE YOUR TEXT]. Tell me: where did you speed up? Where did you slow down? Where did you want to skim? What single sentence would you underline? What single sentence almost lost you? What did you expect to happen that didn't? Give me this as a real reader experience, not an editorial checklist.
Why it works: Writers read their own work as they meant it to sound. AI reads it as it actually reads — and that gap is where most problems live.

⚡Beat Writer's Block (Prompts 28–30)

Prompt 28
Start anywhere but the beginning
I need to write [TYPE OF PIECE] but I'm stuck on the opening. Instead of starting at the beginning, write me: the most dramatic sentence from somewhere in the middle, the last sentence of the piece (as if it's already written), and the single most important thing a reader should feel after finishing. Now — based on those three pieces — write me 5 possible first sentences. I'll pick one and use it to start.
Why it works: The opening is the hardest sentence because it carries the weight of everything that follows. Starting from the middle removes that weight.
Prompt 29
Write badly on purpose
Write a terrible first draft of [WHAT I'M TRYING TO WRITE]. Make it: clichéd, overwritten, full of hedging language, passive voice, obvious observations. Go full "bad writer" mode. Then, in a second pass, take the same content and rewrite it as cleanly and specifically as possible — no wasted words, active voice, specific details. The contrast between the two versions will show me exactly what to cut from my own draft.
Why it works: Writer's block is often perfectionism in disguise. Seeing what "bad" looks like gives you permission to start — and a clear target to aim away from.
Prompt 30
Interview yourself out of the block
I'm trying to write about [TOPIC] but I keep stalling. Act as a journalist interviewing me. Ask me 8 questions that would help me uncover what I actually think about this topic — not what I'm "supposed" to think. Go for the unexpected question, the uncomfortable follow-up, the "but why do YOU actually care about this?" After the interview, use my answers to write the first 200 words of the piece in my voice.
Why it works: Most writer's block is a clarity problem, not a creative problem. You don't know what you think yet. This interview surfaces it.
