25 Free ChatGPT Prompts for Job Seekers (Resume, Cover Letter, Interviews) 2026

By PromptFlow April 2026 10 min read Free to use
Over 30% of job seekers now use AI to help with applications — but most are using it wrong. They paste their resume into ChatGPT and ask it to "make it better." What they get back is generic, forgettable, and often gets flagged by ATS (applicant tracking systems). These 25 prompts show you how to use AI as a research and editing partner — not a ghostwriter. Replace anything in [BRACKETS] with your specifics. Works with ChatGPT (free or Plus), Claude, and Gemini.

📄Resume Optimization (Prompts 1–7)

Prompt 1
I'm applying for a [job title] role at [company type or specific company]. Here is the job description: [paste job description]. Analyze this JD and give me: (1) The 5-7 most important skills/qualifications the employer is prioritizing (rank them), (2) The exact keywords I must include in my resume to pass ATS screening — list them, (3) Any "dealbreaker" requirements I should make sure are clearly visible at the top of my resume, (4) What this company is probably most worried about in a candidate (what risk are they trying to avoid?), (5) What tone does this company seem to value — formal/technical/startup casual/corporate? This tells me how to frame my experience.
Most people apply to jobs without actually analyzing the JD. This 5-minute exercise tells you exactly what to emphasize before you write a single word — and saves you from tailoring your resume around the wrong priorities.
Prompt 2
Here is one bullet point from my resume: "[paste your bullet]" Rewrite this bullet to: (1) Start with a strong action verb (not "Responsible for" or "Helped with"), (2) Include a metric or result if possible — if I didn't track exact numbers, suggest what type of metric would be believable and ask me to fill in a realistic estimate, (3) Make the impact explicit — what changed because of this work?, (4) Keep it under 20 words. Give me 3 different versions using different action verbs and structures. Explain what makes each version stronger than my original.
Weak verbs and missing metrics are the two most common resume problems. Reviewers spend 7-10 seconds on first pass — a bullet with "Managed quarterly reports" vs. "Reduced reporting time by 40% by automating quarterly data pipeline" communicates completely different competence levels.
Prompt 3
Here is my resume (paste below) and here is the job description I'm targeting (paste below): [RESUME] [JOB DESCRIPTION] Do a gap analysis: (1) Which requirements from the JD are NOT represented in my resume?, (2) Which of my skills or experiences ARE relevant but not obvious from how I've written them?, (3) What are the top 3 changes I should make to better match this specific role?, (4) Are there any ATS keywords in the JD that are completely absent from my resume?, (5) What should I add to my resume summary/objective to immediately signal fit for this role? Do NOT rewrite my resume. Give me specific, actionable edits I can make myself.
A tailored resume isn't about lying — it's about surfacing the relevant parts of your experience that a generic resume buries. Most applicants have the experience; they just haven't framed it in the language the hiring manager is using.
Prompt 4
Write a resume summary statement for me. My situation: I am a [describe: recent grad / career changer / experienced professional with X years in Y field]. I'm targeting roles as [job title/field]. My strongest qualifications for this type of role are: [list 2-3 specific things — skills, experiences, accomplishments]. What makes me different from other candidates is: [anything distinctive]. Write 3 summary options: (1) one that leads with my most impressive credential or accomplishment, (2) one that leads with what I bring to the specific type of role I'm targeting, (3) one for career changers that bridges my previous experience to the new direction. Under 50 words each. No buzzwords like "dynamic" or "results-driven" — be specific.
Resume summaries fail when they describe the kind of candidate the applicant wants to be, not the specific value they bring. "Results-driven marketing professional" says nothing. "Former teacher turned B2B marketer who grew email open rates from 18% to 34%" says everything.
Prompt 5
I'm making a career change from [current/previous field] to [target field]. Here is my background: [brief description of experience and skills]. Here is the type of role I'm targeting: [describe]. Help me identify: (1) Which skills from my background transfer directly to the target field (list them with a "translation" — how they map), (2) Which accomplishments from my old career are most impressive to someone in the new field, (3) How should I frame my "why I'm changing" to address the obvious employer concern (why are they leaving their previous career?), (4) What new skills or credentials would most credibly fill the gap? (5) Write a resume summary that positions my background as an asset, not a liability.
Career changers fail at resume writing because they describe what they did (irrelevant to the new role) instead of what they're capable of (often very relevant). This prompt forces the translation exercise that makes a career change resume actually work.
Prompt 6
Scan my resume for these common problems and tell me exactly what to fix: (1) Any use of weak or passive verbs (was, were, helped, assisted, responsible for, worked on, participated in), (2) Bullets without metrics or results — flag each one and suggest what type of metric would strengthen it, (3) Any clichés or buzzwords (passionate, driven, dynamic, synergy, leverage) — suggest replacements, (4) Formatting issues that might cause ATS problems (tables, images, unusual fonts, columns), (5) Anything that sounds like it was written by AI rather than a human. [PASTE RESUME HERE]
Most people can't see their own resume's problems because they're too close to it. This checklist catches the specific patterns that hiring managers have trained themselves to notice — and lose interest at.
Prompt 7
I have a [gap in employment / short job tenure / being laid off / fired / overqualified for the role I'm applying for] that I need to address on my resume or in an interview. The situation is: [describe honestly what happened]. Help me: (1) Decide whether to address this proactively on the resume or wait for the interview, (2) Write a brief, confident answer to "can you explain this gap?" or "why did you leave so quickly?" or [the specific awkward question], (3) What's the one-sentence version I can give if someone asks casually? (4) What should I NOT say, even if it's true?, (5) What positive framing is honest but shifts the focus to where I'm going, not where I've been?
Resume gaps and awkward situations derail candidates who haven't practiced the response. Preparing a calm, honest, forward-focused answer in advance — rather than improvising under pressure — is the difference between sounding confident and sounding defensive.

✉️Cover Letters That Actually Get Read (Prompts 8–12)

Prompt 8
I need to write a cover letter for a [job title] role at [company name]. Here is the job description: [paste JD]. Here is my relevant background: [2-3 sentences on your experience]. Here is something specific about this company that genuinely interests me: [be specific — a product, initiative, mission, or recent news — not just "I've always admired your company"]. Write a cover letter that: (1) Opens with something other than "I am writing to apply for..." — use a specific hook, (2) Connects one specific thing about the company to one specific thing about my background, (3) Addresses the biggest qualification they're looking for with a concrete example from my experience, (4) Ends with a clear ask (a call, an interview) without sounding desperate. Under 300 words. Sounds like a human wrote it.
Cover letters fail for two reasons: they repeat the resume (boring) or they praise the company in vague terms (unconvincing). The key is specificity — one real connection between who they are and what you've done.
Prompt 9
Here is a cover letter I wrote: [paste your draft]. Give me specific feedback: (1) Does the opening sentence make someone want to keep reading — or is it forgettable?, (2) Is there a specific, concrete claim I make about my experience — or is it all vague?, (3) Does the letter sound like it was written for THIS company or could it be sent to 50 companies unchanged?, (4) Does it sound like a human or like AI wrote it? Identify the specific sentences that feel generic or AI-generated., (5) What is the single most important change that would most improve this letter? Do NOT rewrite it. Tell me specifically what to change and why.
Most AI-generated cover letters have the same tells: over-formal language, vague enthusiasm, and a structure that mirrors the job description. A critique of your draft — rather than a rewrite — keeps your voice while fixing the specific weaknesses.
Prompt 10
I'm applying for a role where I don't meet all the requirements. The role requires [list requirements]. I have [describe what you DO have]. I'm missing [describe what you're lacking — years of experience, a specific skill, a degree, etc.]. Write a cover letter that: (1) Leads with the most relevant thing I DO have, (2) Addresses the gap directly but briefly — don't ignore it, (3) Reframes my different background as something that actually adds value (not just "compensates"), (4) Ends by making it easy for them to say yes to an interview — not an offer. Under 280 words. Note: I want to be honest about the gap — no exaggerating or false claims.
Applying for stretch roles is a numbers game that requires a different cover letter strategy. Ignoring the gap reads as oblivious; obsessing over it reads as insecure. Acknowledging it briefly and pivoting to value is the confident play.
Prompt 11
I want to reach out to [person's name or role] at [company] about a potential opportunity. This is a cold email — there is no open job posting. My goal is to get a conversation, not a job offer. Context: [why you're reaching out to this company specifically / what you know about their work / any connection point]. My background: [1-2 sentences]. Write a cold outreach email that: (1) Shows I've done research — not a generic pitch, (2) Is under 100 words (respect their time), (3) Makes a small, specific ask (a 15-minute call — not "pick your brain" which is too vague and undervalues their time), (4) Gives them one clear reason to say yes. Subject line included.
Cold outreach emails fail because they're too long, too vague, or ask for too much. The ideal cold email takes 30 seconds to read and makes a small enough ask that the cost of saying yes is minimal.
Prompt 12
I got a rejection email from [company] for a [role] I really wanted. I want to respond in a way that: (1) stays professional and doesn't burn the bridge, (2) expresses genuine interest in future opportunities, (3) asks for feedback in a way that's likely to get a real response (not a form email), (4) leaves them with a positive impression of me. Here is the rejection email: [paste it] Write a response under 80 words. Also: is it ever worth asking to be reconsidered if you have new information, and how would you phrase that?
Responding to rejections is something almost no one does — which means the people who do it stand out immediately. The hiring manager who rejected you for this role will remember you for the next one.

💼LinkedIn Profile Optimization (Prompts 13–17)

Prompt 13
Write a LinkedIn headline for me. I am a [describe your role/background]. I'm targeting roles as [target job title]. What makes me distinctive is: [1-2 specific things — not vague strengths like "results-driven" — e.g., "7 years in B2B SaaS sales, closed $4M ARR last year" or "UX designer specializing in fintech onboarding flows"]. Write 5 headline options under 120 characters each. Each should use a different formula: (1) Role + niche + outcome, (2) What you help people/companies do, (3) Current role + career goal signal, (4) Credentials + outcome, (5) Problem you solve. Rate each for searchability (will recruiters find me?) vs. click-ability (will they want to learn more?).
Your LinkedIn headline appears in search results, connection requests, and email previews. "Marketing Manager at XYZ Corp" is a missed opportunity — every competitor has the same headline. One specific outcome or specialty makes you memorable in 3 seconds.
Prompt 14
Write a LinkedIn About section for me. Here is my background: [describe your career arc — where you started, where you are now, major roles or transitions]. Here is what I'm best at: [2-3 specific skills or domains]. Here is what I want to be known for: [what you want recruiters or connections to remember]. I'm targeting: [type of role or company]. Write an About section that: (1) Opens with a first-person sentence that immediately signals my value (not "I am an experienced..."), (2) Tells my career story in a way that makes the current role make sense, (3) Is specific — at least one metric or concrete example, (4) Ends with a clear "what I'm looking for" or "feel free to reach out if...", (5) Is under 250 words. Conversational, not corporate.
LinkedIn Abouts are usually either a resume in paragraph form (boring) or a vague narrative about passion (unconvincing). The sweet spot: a story that shows progression + one concrete achievement + a clear signal of where you're headed.
Prompt 15
I want to optimize my LinkedIn profile for recruiters searching for [job title]. My current profile is [describe briefly or paste the current About/headline]. Give me: (1) The top 10 keywords a recruiter searching for this role is likely to use — I need to include these naturally in my profile, (2) Which sections of my profile are most important for recruiter search visibility (ranked), (3) What the "Open to Work" settings should look like — what job titles and locations to include, (4) One specific change I could make to my profile today that would most improve my recruiter visibility, (5) Are there any profile red flags that might cause a recruiter to pass — like gaps, unusual formatting, or missing sections?
LinkedIn's search algorithm weights certain sections more heavily than others (headline and current title most, skills section third). Most profiles are optimized by intuition rather than by understanding how the algorithm actually works.
Prompt 16
Write a LinkedIn connection request message for each of these scenarios (under 300 characters each — LinkedIn's limit for connection notes): Scenario 1: Reaching out to a recruiter at a company I want to work for (no mutual connection) Scenario 2: Following up with someone I met briefly at an event or online Scenario 3: Reaching out to someone in a role I want to transition into, to learn about their path Scenario 4: Connecting with a former colleague I've lost touch with Scenario 5: Reaching out to a hiring manager (not the recruiter) at a company I'm applying to For each: write the note, explain why this approach works, and note what NOT to do (the most common mistake for that scenario).
Connection requests without notes are accepted at around 30-40%. Personalized notes — especially ones that are specific and don't immediately ask for something — can get 70-80% acceptance. 300 characters is tight; every word matters.
Prompt 17
I want to write a LinkedIn post that builds my professional credibility and attracts opportunities. My field is [describe]. My experience level is [describe]. What I want to be known for: [describe your niche or expertise]. Write 3 different post ideas with full drafts: (1) A lesson I learned from a specific work experience (story format — don't make it a listicle), (2) An insight about my industry or field that's non-obvious — something that would make someone in my field say "that's a good point," (3) A career pivot or challenge I overcame (vulnerability + lesson = highly engaging format). Each post: under 200 words, conversational, ends with a question that invites genuine responses. No hashtag spam.
LinkedIn posts that grow professional opportunities are specific, opinionated, and story-driven. The exact opposite of what most people write: generic advice in list format with 15 hashtags. One good post per week outperforms five generic ones.

🎯Interview Preparation & Negotiation (Prompts 18–25)

Prompt 18
I have an interview for a [job title] role at [company]. The interview format is [describe: phone screen / first-round panel / technical / case study / final round]. Here is the job description: [paste]. Give me: (1) The 8 questions I'm most likely to be asked at this stage (include at least 2 behavioral questions), (2) For the behavioral questions: the STAR format (Situation, Task, Action, Result) — suggest what type of story from my background would work for each, (3) The 3 questions the interviewer is really trying to answer with their "official" questions — knowing the underlying concern helps me answer more strategically, (4) 3 smart questions I should ask at the end that show I've done my research, (5) One thing most candidates fail to do in this type of interview that I should do.
Interviewers ask consistent questions because they're trying to assess consistent things. Understanding the underlying concern behind "tell me about a challenge you faced" (they want to see self-awareness and resilience, not just a hard story) completely changes the quality of your answer.
Prompt 19
I'm preparing my answer to "Tell me about yourself" for an interview for a [job title] at [company type]. My background: [describe in 3-4 sentences]. My strongest qualifications for this specific role: [list 2-3 things]. Write 3 versions of a "Tell me about yourself" answer: (1) Under 60 seconds — the efficient version for a recruiter screen, (2) 90 seconds — for a hiring manager who wants more context, (3) For someone who is career-changing — how to tell the story so the transition makes sense. Each version should: end by connecting to why I'm interested in THIS role at THIS company, NOT just summarize my resume chronologically.
"Tell me about yourself" is the most important 90 seconds of any interview and the one people practice least. A poor answer doesn't disqualify you, but a great answer sets the entire tone — you're the one who controls this answer completely, unlike everything else.
Prompt 20
I need to answer this behavioral interview question: "[paste the question]" using the STAR format. Here are 2-3 real stories from my experience that might be relevant: [describe them briefly — what happened, what you did, what the result was]. Help me: (1) Which of my stories best fits this question?, (2) Rewrite the story in STAR format — make the Action section specific and detailed (this is where most answers go weak), (3) Make sure the Result includes a metric or measurable outcome (even an estimated one), (4) Keep it under 2 minutes when spoken out loud (roughly 250-300 words), (5) What follow-up question is the interviewer likely to ask after my answer? Prepare a one-sentence response to it.
Behavioral questions ("Tell me about a time you...") are predictable. Preparing 6-8 flexible stories that can be adapted to different questions is the single most efficient interview preparation strategy — the same story can answer "leadership," "conflict," and "problem-solving" questions depending on what you emphasize.
Prompt 21
I'm being asked to complete a take-home assignment / case study as part of my interview process. Here is what they've asked me to do: [describe the assignment]. The company is [describe]. The role is [job title]. Help me think through this strategically: (1) What is this company most likely evaluating with this specific assignment?, (2) What are the most common mistakes candidates make on this type of assignment?, (3) What format and length are appropriate — and what's the unspoken expectation they have that most candidates miss?, (4) What assumptions should I state upfront (rather than getting lost in speculation)?, (5) Review my draft [paste if you have one] and tell me the top 3 improvements that would make it stronger.
Take-home assignments are evaluated on judgment and communication, not just correct answers. Understanding what they're actually testing — before you start — helps you prioritize the right parts instead of spending 6 hours on something they'll scan for 3 minutes.
Prompt 22
A company just gave me a verbal offer for a [job title] at [company type]. The offer is: Salary: $[X], plus [list any bonuses, equity, benefits]. The market rate for this role is approximately $[Y] based on [what research you've done]. I want to negotiate. Help me: (1) What is a realistic target number to negotiate to? (give me a range and explain), (2) Write the exact words I should say when I respond to the offer — in spoken form, because I want to practice it, (3) What are the non-salary items I should try to negotiate if they won't move on salary? (list them in priority order), (4) What is the one mistake that kills negotiations that I must avoid?, (5) If they say "this is our best offer" — what do I say?
Salary negotiation is statistically one of the highest-ROI activities in a career. A 10-minute conversation can add $5,000-15,000 to your first-year salary — and because future raises are often percentage-based, the compounding effect is massive. Most people don't negotiate because they don't know what to say. This prompt removes that obstacle.
Prompt 23
I received a job offer and I need to decide whether to take it. The offer: [describe role, salary, company]. My current situation: [describe: employed/unemployed, current salary, other offers in play]. My priorities are: [list your actual priorities — salary, growth, stability, work-life balance, mission, whatever matters to you]. Help me make this decision: (1) A structured comparison of what this offer gives me vs. what I'd be giving up, (2) What questions I should ask the company before deciding (that I haven't asked yet), (3) What would a "red flag" look like in their response to my questions?, (4) Is the offer timeline appropriate, or is it pressure designed to prevent me from comparing options?, (5) What information would change my decision if I found it out?
Job offer decisions are emotionally loaded and time-pressured — the exact conditions that produce regretted decisions. A structured decision framework surfaces the real trade-offs before you're committed, not after.
Prompt 24
I have an interview with [describe the interviewer: CEO / HR / hiring manager / panel / technical lead] at [company type]. I want to research this person before the interview. Tell me: (1) What are the best sources to find information about this person before an interview?, (2) What specifically should I be looking for? (not just "read their LinkedIn" — what am I actually trying to learn), (3) Based on their role as [interviewer's title], what are they personally evaluating me on? What matters most to them specifically vs. what matters to the hiring manager or recruiter?, (4) What kind of questions does a [interviewer's title] typically ask that are different from standard interview questions?, (5) How should I reference my research during the interview without it feeling creepy or rehearsed?
Different interviewers are evaluating different things. A CEO interviewing a final candidate cares about culture fit and ambition; a technical lead cares about depth and problem-solving. Knowing what each interviewer is actually assessing — before you walk in — lets you tailor your answers to the right audience.
Prompt 25
I want to write a thank-you email after my interview with [describe who you interviewed with] at [company]. The interview was [describe: went well / had a rough moment / was competitive / was very conversational]. A specific thing we discussed that I want to reference: [describe one specific topic or moment from the interview]. Write a thank-you email that: (1) Is sent within 2 hours of the interview, (2) References something specific from our conversation — NOT generic ("I enjoyed learning about your company"), (3) Adds one brief point I wish I'd said or elaborated on during the interview, (4) Reaffirms my interest without sounding desperate, (5) Is under 120 words. Subject line included.
Thank-you emails are read. Recruiters have confirmed this. The ones that get remembered reference something specific from the interview — which proves you were actually listening, not just waiting for your turn to talk. The ones that don't get remembered start with "Thank you for taking the time."
On using AI for job applications: AI is a great writing partner but a poor ghostwriter. Recruiters and hiring managers are increasingly good at spotting fully AI-written cover letters and LinkedIn profiles — they feel vague, over-polished, and impersonal. The right approach: use these prompts to structure your thinking, draft a starting point, and edit aggressively in your own voice. The most effective applications are specific, human, and clearly written by someone who's thought carefully about the role. AI helps you get there faster — it doesn't replace the thinking.
