📣Ad Copy Prompts

Prompt 1
Facebook/Instagram Ad — AIDA Framework
Write a Facebook ad for [PRODUCT/SERVICE] targeting [TARGET AUDIENCE: e.g., female entrepreneurs aged 28-45 interested in productivity]. Use the AIDA framework: Attention (hook that names their specific problem), Interest (why this solution is different), Desire (specific outcome they'll get), Action (CTA). Keep the body copy under 125 words. Headline under 40 characters. CTA button text: [e.g., Shop Now / Learn More / Get Started].
Why it works: AIDA is the most tested direct-response framework. Specifying the audience and character limits forces output that's actually usable, not editorial.
Prompt 2
Google Search Ad — 3 Headlines + 2 Descriptions
Write Google Search ad copy for [PRODUCT/SERVICE]. Target keyword: "[KEYWORD]". Write 3 headlines (max 30 characters each) that include the keyword and emphasize different angles: (1) problem-focused, (2) benefit-focused, (3) urgency or social proof. Write 2 descriptions (max 90 characters each): the first should expand on the main benefit, the second should have a clear CTA. Do not exceed the character limits.
Why it works: Google RSAs test headline combinations automatically. Giving three distinct angles maximizes variation and performance data.
Prompt 3
Ad Copy Variations — A/B Testing Set
Write 3 variations of ad copy for [PRODUCT/SERVICE] targeting [AUDIENCE]. Each variation should test a different hook angle: Version A — lead with the problem ("[PAIN POINT]"). Version B — lead with the outcome ("[DESIRED RESULT]"). Version C — lead with social proof or a surprising number ("[STAT or CLAIM]"). Keep each version under 100 words. Same CTA for all three: [CTA TEXT].
Why it works: Testing one variable (hook angle) while keeping everything else constant gives you real conversion data, not opinion.
Prompt 4
Retargeting Ad — "You Visited, Here's Why to Come Back"
Write a retargeting ad for [PRODUCT/SERVICE] for people who visited [PAGE/SITE] but didn't purchase. Acknowledge they've seen it before without being creepy. Address the most likely objection: [OBJECTION — e.g., price, not sure if right for me, forgot about it]. Offer a reason to return: [INCENTIVE — e.g., limited stock, customer reviews, free trial]. Max 80 words body copy. CTA: [CTA TEXT].
Why it works: Retargeting copy should reference the prior visit and handle the specific objection that stopped the first conversion.

📧Email Marketing Prompts

Prompt 5
Subject Line — 5 Variations to A/B Test
Write 5 email subject lines for an email about [TOPIC/OFFER] going to [AUDIENCE]. Write one of each type: (1) Curiosity gap — incomplete information that demands opening. (2) Specificity — a number or specific detail. (3) Direct benefit — what they get. (4) Personal/conversational — sounds like it's from a real person. (5) Urgency — time or scarcity. Under 50 characters each. No all-caps. No spam trigger words.
Why it works: Testing subject line angles in batches reveals which style resonates with your specific list. Most marketers A/B test two — testing five gives 5× the learning.
Prompt 6
Promotional Email — Product Launch or Sale
Write a promotional email announcing [PRODUCT/OFFER] to [AUDIENCE SEGMENT]. Structure: (1) Subject line — curiosity or direct benefit. (2) Opening sentence — one sentence that earns the next read. (3) The offer — what it is, the price, what they get. (4) Three specific benefits — not features, outcomes the reader will experience. (5) Urgency — why act now. (6) CTA — one link, one action. Max 250 words total. Tone: [e.g., direct and conversational / professional / enthusiastic].
Why it works: Structured briefing prevents the AI from writing a meandering email. The constraint of one CTA forces clarity.
Prompt 7
Re-Engagement Email — Win Back Inactive Subscribers
Write a re-engagement email for subscribers who haven't opened in [TIMEFRAME — e.g., 90 days]. Don't be passive-aggressive. Acknowledge the silence without apology. Offer something worth opening: [VALUE — e.g., a free resource, an update, a discount]. Include one clear question or CTA that requires minimal effort to respond to. Subject line should acknowledge the gap honestly. Max 150 words.
Why it works: Re-engagement emails that acknowledge the silence and deliver immediate value have 2-3× the click rate of generic "we miss you" emails.
Prompt 8
Email Nurture Sequence — 3-Part Mini Series
Write a 3-email nurture sequence for [AUDIENCE] who downloaded [LEAD MAGNET] and haven't purchased [PRODUCT]. Email 1 (Day 1): Deliver the promised value + one useful tip they didn't expect. Email 2 (Day 3): Share a problem case study or before/after example relevant to [TARGET OUTCOME]. Email 3 (Day 7): Make the ask — introduce [PRODUCT], explain how it gets them from where they are to [DESIRED OUTCOME]. CTA in Email 3 only. Under 200 words per email.
Why it works: Three-email sequences that lead with value before asking consistently outperform single-pitch emails. The progression earns the ask.

📱Social Media Prompts

Prompt 9
LinkedIn Post — Thought Leadership
Write a LinkedIn post about [TOPIC/LESSON/OPINION] in my voice. Context about me: [YOUR ROLE, EXPERIENCE, PERSPECTIVE]. The post should: (1) Open with a counterintuitive statement or surprising observation — no generic hooks. (2) Share a specific insight or lesson, not general advice. (3) End with a question or reflection that invites comments. Target length: 150-200 words. No bullet points. Write in first person. No corporate jargon.
Why it works: LinkedIn's algorithm rewards native long-form text with high dwell time. Counterintuitive openings stop the scroll. A question at the end drives comments, which expands reach.
Prompt 10
Instagram Caption — Carousel or Single Image
Write an Instagram caption for a [CAROUSEL/SINGLE IMAGE] post about [TOPIC] for [AUDIENCE — e.g., freelancers, small business owners]. Hook (first line, under 125 characters — this is what shows before "more"): [one-line version of the main insight or an intriguing question]. Body: 3-4 short paragraphs expanding the insight. CTA: Ask a specific question or direct to the link in bio. Add 5-8 relevant hashtags at the end. Tone: [conversational / educational / motivational].
Why it works: Instagram shows only the first 125 characters before "more." The hook is everything — it determines whether anyone reads the rest.
Prompt 11
Twitter/X Thread — Educational Breakdown
Write a Twitter/X thread about [TOPIC] that educates [AUDIENCE] on [SPECIFIC SKILL OR CONCEPT]. Structure: Tweet 1 — hook that promises a specific outcome from reading the thread. Tweets 2-8 — one insight or step per tweet, each under 280 characters, each able to stand alone. Tweet 9 — summary of the key takeaway. Tweet 10 — CTA (follow for more / link to [RESOURCE]). No filler tweets. No "RT if you agree." Start every tweet with a strong opening word, not "I" or "The."
Why it works: Threads that teach a specific skill perform best on X. The "each tweet stands alone" constraint prevents filler padding that kills retention.
Prompt 12
Social Media Content Calendar — One Week
Create a 5-post social media content calendar for [PLATFORM — e.g., LinkedIn / Instagram] for a [BUSINESS TYPE] targeting [AUDIENCE]. Content pillars: [PILLAR 1 — e.g., educational], [PILLAR 2 — e.g., behind-the-scenes], [PILLAR 3 — e.g., promotional]. For each post: day, pillar, topic, hook (first line), post format (text/image/carousel/video), and CTA. Use a 3:1:1 ratio: 3 educational, 1 promotional, 1 personal/engagement post. Include the actual caption for each post.
Why it works: Planning a full week at once with defined pillars prevents the daily "what should I post?" decision that stalls most marketers.

🔍SEO Content Prompts

Prompt 13
Blog Post Outline — Long-Form SEO Article
Create a detailed outline for a 1,500-word SEO blog post targeting the keyword "[TARGET KEYWORD]". The reader is [AUDIENCE — e.g., small business owners unfamiliar with SEO]. Include: (1) H1 title with keyword in first 60 characters. (2) Meta description under 155 characters with keyword. (3) Introduction structure (hook → problem → what this post covers). (4) 4-6 H2 sections with subheadings. (5) FAQ section with 3 questions Google likely shows in People Also Ask. (6) Conclusion + CTA. Each section should have a 2-sentence description of what to cover.
Why it works: An outline from a well-briefed AI takes 5 minutes and saves 30 minutes of structural thinking. The FAQ section targets Google's People Also Ask box directly.
Prompt 14
Meta Descriptions — Batch of 5
Write meta descriptions for these 5 pages on my website: [LIST 5 PAGE TITLES AND THEIR PRIMARY KEYWORDS]. For each, write a meta description under 155 characters that: includes the primary keyword naturally, communicates the page's unique value, and ends with a call to action or benefit. Do not start any with "Welcome to" or "This page is about." Write them to be clicked, not just indexed.
Why it works: Meta descriptions don't directly affect rankings but dramatically affect click-through rate from search results. Treating them as ad copy instead of summaries doubles CTR.
Prompt 15
Product Description — SEO-Optimized for E-Commerce
Write an SEO product description for [PRODUCT NAME] targeting the keyword "[TARGET KEYWORD]". Audience: [BUYER PERSONA — who buys this and why]. Structure: (1) Opening sentence — one line that names the problem this product solves. (2) 3 bullet points — key features as benefits (what they mean for the buyer, not what they are). (3) One paragraph on who it's for and why it works. (4) CTA. Include the target keyword in the first 100 characters. Total length: 150-200 words. Do not use any of these filler phrases: "game-changer," "revolutionary," "cutting-edge."
Why it works: Banning specific filler phrases is the single fastest way to improve AI-generated product copy quality. The benefit-framing instruction converts better than feature lists.

🎯Brand Voice & Copy Prompts

Prompt 16
Brand Voice Guide — Define Your Tone
Create a concise brand voice guide for [BRAND NAME], a [BUSINESS TYPE] targeting [AUDIENCE]. Based on these 3 words I'd use to describe my brand: [WORD 1], [WORD 2], [WORD 3] — write: (1) A 2-sentence brand voice summary. (2) Three "we sound like / we don't sound like" contrasts. (3) A list of 10 words we use and 10 we avoid. (4) One example sentence showing our voice in action for: a product description, a social post, and a customer email. Format as a document my whole team can follow.
Why it works: A brand voice guide built in 15 minutes is infinitely more useful than no guide. The "we sound like / don't sound like" format is the fastest way to make tone concrete.
Prompt 17
Rewrite for Brand Voice — Fix AI-Sounding Copy
Rewrite the following copy to match this brand voice: [3-WORD BRAND DESCRIPTION — e.g., direct, human, confident]. Remove any phrases that sound like they were written by AI: "delve into," "in today's fast-paced world," "it's worth noting," "game-changer," "leverage," "utilize." Replace passive constructions with active ones. Shorten any sentence over 20 words. The goal is copy that sounds like a smart human wrote it on purpose — not copy that sounds like it was generated. [PASTE COPY TO REWRITE HERE]
Why it works: This prompt is the fastest way to rescue AI-generated first drafts. The specific banned-phrases list does more work than any general "make it sound human" instruction.
Prompt 18
Landing Page Headline — 10 Variations
Write 10 landing page headline variations for [PRODUCT/SERVICE] targeting [AUDIENCE]. The main benefit: [BENEFIT]. The main problem it solves: [PROBLEM]. Write two of each type: (1) Problem-focused — names the pain. (2) Benefit-focused — promises the outcome. (3) How-to — implies a method or shortcut. (4) Question — creates a "yes" response. (5) Contrarian — challenges a common belief. Under 10 words each where possible. Do not use the word "transform."
Why it works: 10 variations across 5 angles surfaces options a single prompt never would. Landing page headline is the highest-leverage copywriting decision — worth spending 10 minutes on variations.
Prompt 19
Customer Testimonial Request — Email + Follow-Up
Write two emails: (1) A post-purchase email asking [CUSTOMER TYPE] for a review/testimonial. Send [TIMEFRAME — e.g., 7 days] after purchase. Make it feel personal, not automated. Ask one specific question: "What was the moment you realized [PRODUCT] was working for you?" rather than "How are you enjoying the product?" Under 100 words. (2) A 7-day follow-up if no response — shorter, lower friction. Both emails should make the ask feel easy and genuinely valuable to them to answer.
Why it works: Specific questions get specific testimonials. "What was the moment..." produces a story. "How are you enjoying it?" produces a rating.
Prompt 20
Objection Handler — Copy That Addresses the "No"
Write copy that handles the top 3 objections for [PRODUCT/SERVICE]. The objections are: (1) [OBJECTION 1 — e.g., "It's too expensive"]. (2) [OBJECTION 2 — e.g., "I don't have time to implement this"]. (3) [OBJECTION 3 — e.g., "I've tried tools like this before and they didn't work"]. For each: one paragraph (3-4 sentences) that acknowledges the concern, reframes it, and ends with a response that doesn't feel defensive. Use for FAQ sections, sales pages, or ad copy.
Why it works: Objection copy on a sales page removes the friction that stops purchases. Acknowledging concerns builds trust faster than ignoring them.
The 3-Part Prompt Framework Behind All 20 Prompts

Every prompt above follows the same structure: Context (who you are, who the reader is) → Output format (exactly what you want) → Constraints (character limits, banned phrases, required elements). Without all three, ChatGPT defaults to its average — which is generic. With all three, it produces first drafts you can actually use.
