🛍️Product Listing Descriptions (Prompts 1–5)

Prompt 1
Write a product description for [product name]. Key features: [list 5-7 specs or features]. Target customer: [describe who buys this — busy moms, gym enthusiasts, home bakers, etc.]. Platform: [Shopify / Amazon / Etsy]. Tone: [casual and fun / professional / rustic and handcrafted]. Format: 2-3 short paragraphs. Lead with the main benefit the customer experiences, not the product specs. End with a soft CTA.
Spec-first descriptions don't sell. Benefit-first descriptions get added to cart. This prompt forces the right structure every time.
Prompt 2
Write Amazon bullet points for this product: [product name and brief description]. Create exactly 5 bullet points, each starting with a capitalized benefit phrase (e.g., "SAVES 2 HOURS A DAY —"). Cover: primary benefit, key feature, secondary benefit, compatibility/fit, and guarantee/quality. Each bullet should be 1-2 sentences and under 200 characters. Do not start any bullet with "The" or the product name.
Amazon bullet points follow a specific format that converts. Starting with capitalized benefit phrases increases scan readability and A9 algorithm performance.
Prompt 3
Write an Etsy listing title and description for: [product name]. Keywords I want to include: [list 5-8 relevant search terms]. Title should be under 140 characters and front-load the most important keywords. Description: 200-300 words covering what it is, materials/dimensions, who it's for, how they'll use it, and shipping/download info. Add a "Why customers love this" section with 3 bullet points.
Etsy SEO is keyword-driven. Front-loading your title with the right keywords is the single biggest lever for Etsy search visibility.
Prompt 4
Write 5 different product title variations for this item: [describe product]. Each title should lead with a different angle: (1) the primary use case, (2) the target customer type, (3) the key material or quality differentiator, (4) the occasion or gift angle, (5) a problem-solution framing. Keep each under 80 characters (for Shopify SEO titles). I'll pick the best one to test.
Titles are your most testable asset. Five variants in 30 seconds beats spending an hour on one you're not sure about.
Prompt 5
Rewrite this existing product description to improve conversions: [paste your current description]. Keep all accurate facts. Improve: (1) the opening hook — it needs to grab attention in 2 seconds, (2) any passive or weak language (replace "can help" with "helps," "may be useful" with "works"), (3) the closing CTA. Do not add claims that weren't in the original. Show me the original weakness and the fix side by side.
Existing descriptions are often the fastest win — they already rank, just don't convert. This prompt audits and fixes without starting from scratch.

📣Ad Copy (Prompts 6–10)

Prompt 6
Write 3 Facebook/Instagram ad variations for this product: [product name, price, main benefit]. Each ad should have: (1) a hook (first line must stop the scroll — use a question, surprising stat, or bold claim), (2) 2-3 sentences of body copy, (3) a CTA button text option (e.g., "Shop Now," "Get Yours," "Grab the Deal"). Variation 1: problem-focused. Variation 2: social proof/aspiration. Variation 3: curiosity/hook. Keep each under 125 words.
Three creative variations let you A/B test immediately. The problem/aspiration/curiosity framework covers the three main buying triggers.
Prompt 7
Write a Google Shopping ad for: [product name]. Primary keyword: [main keyword]. Create: (1) 3 headline options (30 chars max each), (2) 2 description options (90 chars max each). Headlines should include the primary keyword, a key benefit, and either a price point or urgency element. Descriptions should expand on benefits and include a clear CTA. Follow Google's character limits exactly.
Google Shopping ads live and die on headlines. Including the keyword + benefit + price signal in 30 characters is a precise craft — this prompt handles the constraint automatically.
Prompt 8
Write a TikTok/Reels video script for this product: [product name]. Hook (first 3 seconds): [must be visually actionable — something that looks interesting on camera]. Script format: 30-45 second video. Include: hook, problem setup (5 sec), product reveal and demo points (15 sec), social proof or result (5 sec), CTA (5 sec). Write it as spoken word — casual, direct, no marketing jargon. End with "link in bio" or "comment [word] and I'll send the link."
TikTok and Reels scripts need a different structure than written ads. The hook-problem-reveal-proof-CTA flow is proven for short-form video.
Prompt 9
Write 10 short-form ad hooks for this product: [product name + one-line description]. Each hook should be under 15 words and test a different angle: curiosity, fear of missing out, social proof, transformation, controversy, time savings, price shock, specificity, question format, and bold statement. I'll test these as the first line of Facebook ads, TikTok openers, and email subject lines.
The hook is 80% of ad performance. 10 options across 10 angles gives you a testing runway for weeks without creating new body copy.
Prompt 10
Write a retargeting ad for customers who visited [product page URL or product name] but didn't buy. Possible objection reasons: [price too high / wasn't sure if it was right for them / got distracted / wanted to think about it]. Create 2 versions: (1) a discount offer version (offer: [X% off or free shipping]), (2) a social proof version (highlight: [# of reviews, star rating, or specific customer result]). Each under 100 words.
Retargeting converts 2-5x better than cold traffic. Having both a discount and a social proof version lets you test price sensitivity vs. trust as the conversion lever.

📧Email Marketing (Prompts 11–15)

Prompt 11
Write an abandoned cart email for a customer who left [product name] (price: $[X]) in their cart. Timing: sent 1 hour after abandonment. Include: (1) subject line (under 50 characters, no "You forgot something" clichés), (2) preview text (under 90 characters), (3) email body — acknowledge the cart, lead with the product's main benefit, handle 1 likely objection (price / shipping / uncertainty), and include a direct link back to checkout. Under 150 words body copy.
Abandoned cart emails recover 5-15% of lost sales. The 1-hour send window has the highest open rates. Avoiding "forgot" language feels less accusatory and gets more opens.
Prompt 12
Write a post-purchase email sequence for customers who just bought [product]. Email 1 (immediate): order confirmation + what to expect. Email 2 (3 days later): how to get the most out of [product] — 3 tips or use cases. Email 3 (7 days later): ask for a review + offer [small incentive or just gratitude]. Write all 3 emails. Each should be under 200 words. Subject lines included.
Post-purchase sequences build loyalty and generate reviews. Three-email sequences outperform single "thank you" emails by 40% on review generation.
Prompt 13
Write a promotional email for a [% off / BOGO / free shipping] sale on [product name or store]. Sale runs: [dates]. Subject line options: give me 5. Body: 150 words max. Include: urgency (sale end date), specific discount mechanics, 1 featured product with its benefit, and a single clear CTA button. No countdown timer copy needed — just static urgency language. Tone: excited but not hysterical.
Promotional emails work best with a single CTA and genuine urgency. Multiple offers in one email dilute conversion.
Prompt 14
Write a win-back email for customers who haven't purchased in [90 / 180] days. What I sell: [brief store description]. Offer to win them back: [discount / new product / free gift with purchase / just a check-in]. Subject line should reference time passed without being passive-aggressive. Body: acknowledge the gap, lead with what's new or what they might have missed, present the offer, CTA. Under 175 words.
Win-back emails have 3-4x lower cost per acquisition than cold traffic. Even a 5% win-back rate on a lapsed list pays for itself.
Prompt 15
Write 5 email subject line variations for [campaign type: new product launch / seasonal sale / product restock / customer story / educational content]. My brand voice: [casual / premium / playful / professional]. Target reader: [describe customer]. Test different angles: (1) curiosity, (2) direct benefit, (3) social proof or number, (4) urgency, (5) personalization hook. Under 50 characters each. Mark which angle each uses.
Subject lines are tested at near-zero cost. Five variations gives your email tool enough to run a real A/B test with statistical meaning.

🎧Customer Service Scripts (Prompts 16–20)

Prompt 16
Write a response to this customer complaint: "[paste complaint]." My store policy on this issue: [describe your policy — e.g., "30-day returns, buyer pays shipping," or "digital downloads are non-refundable"]. Goal: (1) acknowledge their frustration genuinely, (2) explain the policy clearly without being defensive, (3) offer the best resolution within my policy, (4) leave them feeling heard even if I can't fully accommodate them. Under 150 words.
How you respond to complaints determines whether customers leave a 1-star review or a 5-star review about your service recovery. This structure handles both the policy and the human moment.
Prompt 17
Write a response to a customer asking "Where is my order?" Their order was placed [X days ago]. Tracking shows: [delivered / in transit / delayed / no update]. If delivered: guide them through checking their mailbox, neighbors, and building office — then offer next steps if still not found. If delayed: acknowledge the frustration, explain what the tracking shows, and give a realistic resolution timeline. Under 125 words.
"Where's my order" is the #1 customer service question for e-commerce. Having pre-written responses for each tracking status saves hours per week.
Prompt 18
Write a response to a negative review that says: "[paste the review]." Respond publicly (this will be visible to all potential buyers). Goals: (1) thank them for the feedback, (2) address their specific concern factually — don't get defensive, (3) offer a concrete resolution (replacement / refund / help), (4) show future buyers that you care and respond. Under 100 words. Do not sound scripted or copy-paste generic.
How you respond to negative reviews influences whether new buyers trust you. 45% of shoppers are more likely to buy from a store that responds thoughtfully to bad reviews.
Prompt 19
Write a refund request response for a customer who says the product "wasn't what they expected." The product description accurately described it. My policy: [describe]. My goal: offer a resolution that makes them feel heard without automatically issuing a full refund. Options to offer: [exchange / store credit / partial refund / return for full refund]. Write a response that presents the best option first, explains how it works, and asks what they'd prefer. Under 150 words.
Vague "not what I expected" complaints are often solvable with the right resolution framing. This response gives the customer agency while steering toward your preferred outcome.
Prompt 20
Write a review request message to send to customers 7 days after delivery. Product purchased: [product name]. Platform I want the review on: [Google / Etsy / Amazon / Shopify]. Tone: genuine and friendly — not robotic or pushy. Reference their specific purchase. Explain why reviews help small businesses (in 1 sentence). Include a direct link placeholder: [REVIEW LINK]. Under 100 words. Write both an email version and a short SMS version.
Reviews are the single biggest conversion lever for e-commerce. A personalized, well-timed review request gets 3-5x more responses than a generic "please leave a review" email.
Pro tip: For any of these prompts, add "Match this style: [paste example of your brand voice]" at the end to get output that sounds like you, not like a generic AI assistant.
