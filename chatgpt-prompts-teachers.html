25 Free ChatGPT Prompts for Teachers (Lesson Plans, Grading, Parent Communication) 2026

By PromptFlow April 2026 9 min read Free to use
Teachers who use AI effectively aren't replacing their judgment — they're getting rid of the mechanical work that steals hours from teaching. Lesson plan drafts, rubric design, parent email templates, differentiation modifications, and student feedback: these are tasks AI handles well. These 25 prompts are designed specifically for K-12 and higher education teachers. Replace anything in [BRACKETS] with your specifics. Works with ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini.

📚Lesson Planning & Curriculum Design (Prompts 1–6)

Prompt 1
Create a detailed lesson plan for [subject] on [topic]. Grade level: [X]. Class length: [X minutes]. Learning objectives: [list 2-3 things students should know or be able to do by the end]. Prior knowledge students have: [what they already know]. Standards alignment (if needed): [e.g., CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RI.6.1 or just "grade 6 state standards"]. Structure the lesson plan with: (1) learning objectives (2-3, measurable), (2) materials needed, (3) hook/opening (5-10 min) — something that creates curiosity or connects to prior knowledge, (4) main instruction (step by step), (5) guided practice activity, (6) independent practice or assessment, (7) closure (how to wrap up and check understanding), (8) differentiation notes for advanced and struggling students, (9) homework if applicable.
A complete lesson plan framework reduces prep time from 2 hours to 20 minutes. Crucially, it includes differentiation — the part most teachers skip when rushed but that determines whether struggling students fall further behind.
Prompt 2
I'm teaching [topic] to [grade level] students and I need to explain [specific concept that students typically find difficult]. Help me: (1) Explain this concept in the simplest possible terms — assume no prior knowledge, (2) Give me 3 different analogies or real-world examples that would resonate with [grade level] students, (3) Identify the most common misconception students have about this concept and how to address it proactively, (4) Write a 2-minute "concept check" question I can ask mid-lesson to see if students are following, (5) Suggest a hands-on activity or visual that would help kinesthetic or visual learners grasp this.
The hardest part of teaching a familiar concept is explaining it to someone who doesn't know it yet — experts develop a "curse of knowledge" that makes explanation harder, not easier. Three different analogies give you options for different learners.
Prompt 3
Create a week-long unit plan for [subject] on [unit topic] for [grade level]. The unit runs [X days] with [X minutes] per class. Standards to address: [list key standards or learning goals]. What students already know: [describe prior knowledge]. For each day, outline: (1) the day's learning objective, (2) the main activity or instruction, (3) formative check (how you'll know students are learning), (4) materials or prep needed. At the end of the unit plan, include: the summative assessment type (test, project, essay, presentation) that would best measure all learning objectives, and a simple rubric for it.
Unit planning — mapping how concepts connect across multiple lessons — is the most time-consuming planning task. Getting a structured scaffold in 5 minutes leaves the rest of your time for the parts that actually require teacher expertise: modifying it for your specific students.
Prompt 4
I have [X minutes] for a substitute teacher to cover my [subject] class. Grade level: [X]. The sub has [no / basic / good] subject knowledge. Students are currently working on [describe where they are in the unit]. Create a substitute lesson plan that: (1) requires minimal explanation from the sub, (2) keeps students productively engaged without introducing new concepts, (3) includes clear written instructions the sub can read aloud, (4) has a simple task students complete individually or in pairs, (5) includes what to do for early finishers, (6) specifies exactly what the sub should collect at the end. The plan should work even if the sub struggles with the subject matter.
Sub plans written in a rush produce chaotic classrooms. A plan that doesn't rely on the sub's subject knowledge — and includes early-finisher and classroom management components — preserves instructional time even in your absence.
Prompt 5
I need discussion questions for a [subject] lesson on [topic] for [grade level] students. Create 3 tiers of questions following Bloom's Taxonomy: (1) Knowledge/Comprehension — 3 questions that check basic recall and understanding, (2) Application/Analysis — 3 questions that ask students to use what they know or compare ideas, (3) Synthesis/Evaluation — 3 questions that push students to form opinions, make connections, or solve novel problems. For each question: note which level of Bloom's it targets, estimate how much discussion time it's likely to generate, and suggest a follow-up probe if students give a surface-level answer.
Discussion questions that only test recall ("Who was the main character?") produce low-engagement discussions. Tiered questions let you start accessible and escalate — every student can enter at level 1, while advanced students are pushed at level 3.
Prompt 6
I need to create a project-based learning (PBL) unit for [subject] at [grade level] that covers [key standards or skills]. Duration: [X weeks]. Class size: [X students]. Design the PBL unit including: (1) a driving question that's relevant, open-ended, and genuinely interesting to [grade level] students, (2) the real-world scenario or audience that makes the project authentic, (3) the final product or presentation students will create, (4) milestone checkpoints (what they complete at weeks 1, 2, 3…), (5) how to organize student teams, (6) the skills this project builds beyond the subject (collaboration, presentation, research), (7) one major pitfall to anticipate and how to prevent it.
PBL units drive deeper learning than traditional instruction but are complex to design. The "driving question" and "authentic audience" are the two components most teachers underinvest in — and they're what separates PBL that engages students from PBL that just feels like a bigger worksheet.

✏️Assessment Design & Student Feedback (Prompts 7–12)

Prompt 7
Create a grading rubric for [assignment type: essay / presentation / project / lab report / creative work] on [topic] for [grade level] students. The rubric should: (1) have 4-5 criteria that cover the most important aspects of the assignment, (2) use 4 performance levels (Excellent / Proficient / Developing / Beginning — or customize), (3) describe each level in specific, observable terms — not vague words like "good" or "adequate," (4) be something a student could read BEFORE completing the assignment and understand exactly what earns each level. Point values: [total points and whether each criterion is equally weighted or differentiated]. Also include 2-3 common errors to watch for when grading this type of assignment.
Rubrics that use vague language ("shows understanding") don't actually help students improve — they can't act on them. Specific behavioral descriptions ("cites at least 3 sources, at least 1 primary source") give students a clear target and make grading faster and more consistent.
Prompt 8
I just graded a student's [assignment type] on [topic]. Grade level: [X]. Here is a brief description of what the student did well and what was weak: [describe the work in 3-5 sentences]. Write personalized written feedback that: (1) starts with one specific strength (not generic praise like "good job"), (2) identifies the most important area for improvement — just one, not a list, (3) gives a concrete, actionable suggestion for how to improve that specific thing, (4) ends with a forward-looking statement that maintains the student's confidence. Under 100 words. Sounds like a human wrote it, not an AI rubric.
Feedback that lists everything wrong produces anxiety and no improvement. Research consistently shows that students act on feedback most when it's specific, limited to one or two points, and paired with a concrete action step. Ending positively maintains effort and self-efficacy.
Prompt 9
I need to write 5 different versions of feedback for student essays on [topic] at [grade level]. Here are the 5 different performance profiles I need feedback for: 1. High performer who met all criteria but didn't take risks 2. Mid-level student who has a good thesis but weak evidence 3. Struggling student who clearly didn't understand the prompt 4. Student who wrote well but went off-topic 5. Student who has strong ideas but poor grammar/mechanics throughout For each: write 3-4 sentences of written feedback following the "strength → specific improvement → actionable next step" structure. These will be used as templates that I'll customize with student-specific details.
Differentiated feedback templates reduce grading time dramatically while maintaining quality. Having 5 profile-based templates means you're personalizing within a structure rather than writing from scratch for each student — the cognitive load drops significantly.
Prompt 10
Create a quiz on [topic] for [grade level] with [X] questions. Mix of question types: [X] multiple choice, [X] short answer, [X] true/false (optional), [X] extended response (optional). Difficulty: [easy / mixed / challenging]. Standards alignment: [describe or leave blank]. For multiple choice questions: include 4 answer choices where (1) the correct answer is unambiguous, (2) the distractors represent common misconceptions (not just obviously wrong), (3) no "all of the above" or "none of the above." For each question, note: the specific learning objective it tests, the approximate difficulty level, and (for extended response) a sample complete response for a rubric. Provide an answer key at the end.
Well-designed distractors in multiple choice questions reveal exactly which misconceptions students have — each wrong answer tells you something specific. "All of the above" and "none of the above" are lazy question design that don't diagnose learning.
Prompt 11
I notice many students in my [grade level] [subject] class are making this common mistake: [describe the specific error — e.g., "confusing correlation with causation," "not showing their work in multi-step math problems," "writing thesis statements that are too broad"]. Help me: (1) Diagnose WHY students make this mistake (is it a conceptual gap, a procedural error, or a misunderstanding of the expectation?), (2) Write a brief mini-lesson I can deliver in 5-10 minutes to address this directly, (3) Create 2-3 targeted practice examples with explanations, (4) Write a follow-up assessment question that would reveal whether the misconception has been corrected, (5) Suggest how to communicate this to parents without alarming them unnecessarily.
Addressing the root cause of a common error is 5x more efficient than marking the same mistake 30 times individually. A targeted re-teach moment for the whole class — done well — fixes the issue faster than individual feedback on every paper.
Prompt 12
I need to create a self-assessment or peer-assessment form for [assignment type] for [grade level] students. Design the form so that: (1) students can realistically evaluate quality — give them specific, observable criteria (not "was it good?"), (2) it includes 3-5 questions with a rating scale (1-4, not 1-10 — too granular is harder for students), (3) it includes at least one open-ended reflection question: "The one thing I would change if I had more time is...", (4) the peer version includes a "kindness and honesty" framing so feedback is constructive, (5) it takes under 10 minutes to complete. Also: How do you recommend using these results? Should they factor into the grade?
Self-assessment builds metacognitive awareness — the ability to evaluate one's own work — which transfers across all subjects. Students who accurately self-assess perform better over time because they can identify their own gaps before being told.

📧Parent & Administrative Communication (Prompts 13–18)

Prompt 13
Write a parent email for the following situation: [choose or describe one of these — (1) a student is consistently not completing homework, (2) a student's behavior has become disruptive, (3) a student is significantly underperforming, (4) a student has made impressive progress and I want to recognize them, (5) a sensitive issue came up in class involving a student]. Student situation: [brief description of what's happening]. What I've already tried: [describe any previous conversations or interventions]. What I need from the parent: [your ask — a meeting, home reinforcement, awareness only]. Write the email: professional but warm, specific without being alarming, and under 200 words. Include a subject line. No educational jargon. End with a clear next step.
Difficult parent emails take 30-45 minutes to write carefully because the stakes are high and the language matters. A prompt-generated first draft that's professional, specific, and measured gives you 80% of the work — you add the personal details and send.
Prompt 14
Write a newsletter or weekly update to parents for my [grade level] [subject] class. This week we covered: [describe 2-3 topics or activities]. Upcoming: [list what's coming in the next 1-2 weeks — assignments, tests, field trips, projects]. What parents can do to support learning at home: [be specific — e.g., "ask them to explain how to solve a 2-step equation" or "discuss current events related to our immigration unit"]. Format: conversational, friendly, under 300 words. Should feel like a note from a person, not a school form. No jargon. Include one specific thing a student said or did this week that illustrates what they're learning (I'll fill in the real student example — leave a [STUDENT EXAMPLE HERE] placeholder).
Regular, readable parent updates build trust before problems arise. A newsletter that takes 5 minutes to write (vs 45 minutes) is a newsletter that actually gets written and sent — the difference between parents who feel connected and parents who only hear from you when something is wrong.
Prompt 15
I have a parent-teacher conference for [student description — e.g., "a struggling reader who's made some progress but is still behind grade level" / "a high performer who's bored and acting out" / "a student with attendance issues affecting their grades"]. Time limit: [X minutes]. Help me prepare: (1) A brief summary of this student's current status I can open with (I'll insert real data — draft around placeholders like [GRADE AVERAGE], [STRENGTHS], [AREAS OF CONCERN]), (2) 2-3 specific things I want to communicate, (3) 2-3 questions to ask the parent to understand their perspective, (4) A proposed plan of action we can agree on before the meeting ends, (5) How to close the meeting with a clear next step so it doesn't end vaguely.
Parent conferences without structure run over time, miss key points, or end without clear action. A 5-minute prep using a template gets you to a confident, professional meeting that both parents and you leave feeling good about.
Prompt 16
I need to write a report card comment for a student. Student profile: [describe briefly — grade level, subject, general performance, 1-2 specific things they did well or struggled with this marking period]. Comment length required: [X words or characters]. Write 3 different report card comment options: (1) a positive overall performance comment, (2) a "making progress but needs continued work" comment, (3) a comment for a student who is significantly below expectations but showing effort. Requirements for all three: specific (not "Johnny is a pleasure to have in class"), honest without being discouraging, no educational jargon parents might not understand, and constructive — what should this student do to continue growing?
"A pleasure to have in class" is meaningless feedback that parents quickly learn to ignore. Specific, honest comments — even brief ones — give parents real information and demonstrate professional engagement with their child's learning.
Prompt 17
I need to respond to a difficult parent email. Here is what the parent wrote: [paste the email]. The parent is [describe their concern or complaint: concerned about their child's grade / questioning my teaching methods / upset about something that happened in class / requesting special accommodations / escalating a previous issue]. Help me draft a response that: (1) acknowledges their concern genuinely, without admitting fault for things that aren't my fault, (2) gives the factual context they need to understand the situation, (3) is professional and does not escalate, (4) proposes a specific next step (meeting, phone call, follow-up in 2 weeks), (5) has been reviewed for tone — nothing that would sound defensive or dismissive if forwarded to an administrator. Under 200 words unless the situation requires more.
The most dangerous parent emails are ones teachers respond to too quickly while emotionally activated. This prompt creates distance between the emotional trigger and the response — and the "if forwarded to admin" test is the single best professional filter for any parent communication.
Prompt 18
I need to write a referral or accommodation request for a student who I believe may need additional support. Student situation: [describe what you're observing — learning, behavioral, emotional — without diagnosis]. Grade level: [X]. Subject: [X]. Help me: (1) Document what I've observed in objective, behavioral terms (not interpretive — "the student frequently cannot stay on task for more than 5 minutes despite prompting" rather than "the student seems to have ADHD"), (2) List what I've already tried in my classroom and the outcome, (3) Write the formal referral narrative that's factual, specific, and meets documentation standards, (4) Note what NOT to say in a referral that could create legal or procedural issues, (5) Draft the email to the school counselor or special education coordinator requesting a meeting.
Referral documentation must be objective and behavioral — not diagnostic or interpretive — to protect both the student's rights and your professional standing. Getting the language right is important and time-consuming. This prompt helps you document correctly the first time.

🌟Differentiation, IEPs & Inclusive Instruction (Prompts 19–25)

Prompt 19
I have a lesson on [topic] for [grade level]. I need to differentiate it for three levels of learners in the same class. Here is my standard lesson plan (brief version): [describe the main activity or instruction]. Create three versions: (1) Modified version for students reading 1-2 grade levels below — adjust the language, reduce the text density, add visual supports, and scaffold the main task into smaller steps. Keep the same learning goal, (2) On-grade version — the standard lesson as described, (3) Extension version for students who need more challenge — add depth, complexity, or independent inquiry without just giving "more work." Depth over quantity. For each version: what changes and why.
True differentiation isn't three separate lesson plans — it's one learning goal with three entry points. The extension version is where most differentiation fails: giving advanced students 10 more problems instead of a harder problem is quantity, not depth.
Prompt 20
I need to modify [assignment type: reading passage / writing assignment / math worksheet / test] for a student with the following IEP accommodations: [list the accommodations — e.g., "extended time, reduced assignment length, allow typed responses, simplified language, visual aids allowed"]. Here is the original assignment: [describe or paste the assignment]. Create a modified version that: (1) meets all the listed accommodations, (2) maintains the same learning objective — the modification should provide access, not lower the standard, (3) changes the format but not the rigor where possible, (4) notes which parts were modified and why, so I have documentation. Also: Is there anything in the original assignment that might unintentionally disadvantage this student that I haven't mentioned?
IEP modifications are legally required and educationally important — but creating them from scratch for every assignment is prohibitively time-consuming. A modified version that preserves the learning objective (not just makes the work easier) is what actually serves the student and satisfies legal requirements.
Prompt 21
I have an English Language Learner (ELL) student in my [subject] class at [grade level]. Their English proficiency level is approximately [beginning / intermediate / advanced]. The student's home language is [language if known]. I have an upcoming lesson on [topic] that involves [reading / writing / discussion / complex instructions]. Help me: (1) Identify the specific language demands this lesson places on ELL students (vocabulary, syntax, academic language), (2) Suggest 3-4 concrete scaffolds I can provide without singling the student out (visual aids, sentence frames, vocabulary pre-teaching), (3) Modify the key instructions to be linguistically accessible, (4) Write sentence frames for the discussion or writing component that give the student language support without doing the thinking for them, (5) What is one small group or partner work structure that would reduce language anxiety for this student?
ELL accommodations often focus on content simplification when the actual barrier is academic language (terms like "analyze," "justify," "compare and contrast") — not conceptual difficulty. Scaffolding academic language while maintaining content rigor serves ELL students at the right level.
Prompt 22
I'm designing a unit that I want to be more culturally responsive for my classroom. Subject: [X]. Grade level: [X]. Topic: [X]. My students' backgrounds include: [describe demographics — don't need to be specific, just general cultural or linguistic groups represented]. Help me: (1) Identify where the standard curriculum materials on this topic might reflect a narrow cultural perspective, (2) Suggest 2-3 ways to incorporate multiple perspectives or diverse examples without feeling tokenistic or forced, (3) Identify diverse voices, authors, scientists, historical figures, or case studies I could bring in (I'll verify and source them), (4) Suggest discussion questions that connect the content to students' real experiences without putting any student in the position of being a "representative" of their culture.
Culturally responsive teaching improves engagement for all students, not just minority students — seeing yourself in the curriculum is a basic prerequisite for care about the content. The warning against "tokenism" is important: adding one diverse example in a sea of monoculture isn't responsive, it's decorative.
Prompt 23
A student in my class is consistently disengaged — not disruptive, just completely checked out. Sitting quietly but not working, not making eye contact, appearing detached. Grade level: [X]. Subject: [X]. What I know about this student: [brief description — academic performance, anything you know about their home situation or interests, how long this has been going on]. Help me: (1) Generate 3-4 hypotheses about what might be causing the disengagement (academic, social, emotional, environmental), (2) For each hypothesis, suggest one low-stakes intervention I could try without drawing unwanted attention to the student, (3) Write a check-in question I could ask privately in a way that opens conversation without feeling intrusive, (4) What are the warning signs that this student needs more support than a teacher can provide?, (5) How do I document my concerns in a way that creates a paper trail if escalation becomes necessary?
Quiet disengagement is more dangerous than behavioral disruption because it's invisible. Students who "don't cause problems" are often the ones who fall through the cracks. A structured diagnostic approach — rather than waiting and hoping — catches students earlier.
Prompt 24
Help me design a formative assessment strategy for my [grade level] [subject] class that gives me real data on student understanding without being a traditional quiz. I teach [X] students. Available time for assessment: [5 / 10 / 15 minutes]. I want to assess understanding of: [topic or concept]. Suggest 3 different formative assessment formats with: (1) exact instructions for students, (2) what data it gives me (what I'll learn about each student), (3) how long it takes to review (what does 1-minute and 10-minute review of results look like?), (4) how it could be done both in-person and remotely. Make at least one option completely analog (no tech required).
Formative assessment is only useful if you actually have time to use the data. A format that takes 45 minutes to review isn't formative — it's just a smaller summative assessment. The "1-minute review" test reveals whether the strategy is actually practical.
Prompt 25
I want to write a professional development (PD) reflection or goal for my annual evaluation / teaching portfolio. My teaching focus this year has been: [describe: a new strategy you tried, a challenge you worked on, a student population you focused on]. What went well: [brief description]. What didn't work as planned: [brief description]. What I learned: [1-2 key insights]. Help me: (1) Write a reflective paragraph that demonstrates professional growth — specific, honest, and forward-looking, (2) Draft 1-2 SMART professional goals for next year based on my reflection, (3) Identify 2-3 professional development activities (courses, books, communities, conferences) that would support my stated goals — general recommendations I can research further, (4) Write a paragraph that frames my challenges as growth rather than failure — for the portfolio, not for myself.
Teaching portfolios and PD reflections are done at the worst time of year — end of the school year, when teachers are exhausted. Having a structured framework and a draft to work from makes the difference between a thoughtful portfolio entry and a rushed one written in 20 minutes on a Sunday night.
On AI and academic integrity in your classroom: Students are using ChatGPT too — some productively, some to circumvent learning. The most effective response isn't prohibition (largely unenforceable) but designing assessments that require genuine thinking: oral presentations, in-class writing, reflections on personal experience, and assignments that build on unique classroom discussions. These prompts are tools for teacher productivity — the same transparency you bring to your own AI use models the informed, critical use you want to teach.
